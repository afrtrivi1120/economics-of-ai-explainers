# The Economics of Algorithmic Personalization: Evidence from an Educational Technology Platform

**Authors:** Keshav Agrawal, Susan Athey, Ayush Kanodia, Shanjukta Nath, Emil Palikot
**Year:** 2026 (March 2026)
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 34950
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/agrawal-athey-et-al_2026_algorithmic-personalization-edtech.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/agrawal-athey-et-al_2026_algorithmic-personalization-edtech.pdf)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34950

## 1. Full citation

Agrawal, K., Athey, S., Kanodia, A., Nath, S., & Palikot, E. (2026). *The Economics of Algorithmic Personalization: Evidence from an Educational Technology Platform*. NBER Working Paper No. 34950. National Bureau of Economic Research.

## 2. Research question

Personalized recommendation algorithms are economically dominant on entertainment platforms (Netflix, Spotify, YouTube). Educational technology platforms — which suffer notoriously low retention rates of 3-6% completion (p. 2) — have been comparatively slow to adopt them. The paper asks three connected questions. First, can collaborative-filtering personalization meaningfully raise engagement for children using a moderately scaled (~6,400 daily active users) reading app? Second — and most importantly — when an algorithmic intervention is scaled from a controlled experiment to full deployment, do treatment effects shrink (the "voltage drop" common in policy scale-ups), or do they grow because the algorithm learns from more data? Third, what features of the platform — content library size, user history depth — bind the long-run benefits? (p. 2-4).

## 3. Method

- **Setting.** Freedom, the flagship app of Stones2Milestones (S2M), an Indian EdTech firm. Freedom serves children aged 3-12 learning to read English through ~3,200 short illustrated stories organized into horizontally scrollable "slates" of 15 stories per section. Three acquisition channels: B2B partnerships with schools (35%), B2C parents (40%), and paid subscribers (25%) (p. 8).
- **Intervention.** A collaborative-filtering recommendation system replaced editorial curation in one section of the app. An eligibility threshold of **60 cumulative story interactions** was set: users below the threshold received editorial recommendations (cold-start), users above received personalized rankings. The threshold creates the running variable for a regression-discontinuity design (p. 2).
- **Two-phase design.** (1) **Randomized controlled trial** with 7,750 eligible users, treated users receive personalized rankings in one section, controls keep editorial. (2) **Scaled deployment** for five months with all eligible users on personalization, plus a sharp **regression discontinuity** at the 60-interaction threshold to estimate effects continuously (p. 2-3, Figure 1).
- **Engagement metric.** A *Story Engagement* score with partial credit: 0.3 for viewing the story description, 0.5 for starting, 1.0 for completing. Aggregated across all stories per user-week (Y_it = ΣU_ij). The metric reflects that only ~8% of impressions result in completion, with most interactions involving exploration (p. 9).
- **Identification.** RCT relies on standard randomization. RDD adopts the *local-randomization* framework of Cattaneo, Idrobo & Titiunik (2015, 2018) within a window around the threshold. The threshold is mechanical, unknown to users, and shows no treatment-response asymmetry beyond the policy assignment (p. 13-14). Validated through (i) treated/control comparison just below the threshold during the experiment, (ii) week-prior engagement balance, (iii) placebo test in a section that never received personalization (p. 3).
- **Heterogeneity.** Pre-specified subgroup analysis by user-history depth (heavy vs. light users), grade level, and acquisition channel.

## 4. Key findings

- **Personalization raises engagement substantially.** RCT: a **60% increase** (SE 17%) in engagement *within* the personalized section, and a **14% increase** (SE 7%) in *app-wide* engagement (p. 3). The app-wide gain implies users substitute *toward* the app from other activities, not merely shifting time across sections.
- **Reverse voltage drop at scale.** During the five-month scaled deployment, **RDD estimates exceed the RCT benchmark by a factor of ~2.5** (p < 0.001), with substantial week-to-week variation (p. 3). This is the *opposite* of the standard "voltage drop" finding (List 2022) where treatment effects shrink at scale; here the algorithm learns from more deployment data and improves.
- **Heterogeneity by interaction history.** Heavy users (more interaction history) gain **24 percentage points more** than light users in the RCT (p < 0.01) — consistent with collaborative filtering needing data to learn preferences (p. 4). This is a fundamental feature of matrix-factorization recommenders.
- **Effects vary over time.** During the first month of deployment, scaled effects are comparable to the RCT. They then **grow substantially** during mid-deployment, with high week-to-week volatility — particularly during school holidays and Diwali, when baseline app usage drops and several weeks show no detectable gains (p. 4).
- **Front-loaded benefits and content-inventory ceiling.** Engagement gains are largest in users' *first week* of personalization and decline sharply thereafter. Predicted engagement drops by **20%** within the top 20 stories — corresponding to ~3 weeks of usage for an average user (p. 4). The mechanism is content-inventory depletion: the algorithm continues to optimize, but the set of high-quality matches shrinks as users exhaust preferred content. Freedom's library of 3,200 stories partitioned across 8 grade levels imposes a binding ceiling on sustained gains (p. 7-8).
- **Algorithmic and content investment are complements, not substitutes.** A central economic insight: at moderate scale, *algorithmic improvement and content-library development are complements*. Without enough content, even a perfect recommender saturates. Without good recommendations, content gets undiscovered.
- **Engagement vs. learning.** The paper measures engagement, not test scores. Citing Escueta et al. (2020), they note 12 of 19 RCTs that increased EdTech engagement also improved learning outcomes (p. 4). They also document that treated users spend more *time reading* and complete *more difficult* stories, suggesting an engagement-quality mechanism plausibly linked to learning, but they do not test learning directly.

## 5. Policy implications

- **Public-procurement EdTech evaluations.** Governments and donors that buy EdTech for public schools typically rely on RCT estimates to forecast impact at scale. This paper warns that **RCT estimates can be a *lower* bound** for algorithmic interventions: as data accumulates, the algorithm improves. Standard "voltage drop" discounts are not appropriate for algorithm-driven products. Procurement frameworks should treat RCT estimates as a floor, not a ceiling (p. 4).
- **Content investment is the binding constraint.** For ministries of education or NGOs deploying reading or math apps, the authors' result implies that *expanding* the content library is at least as important as algorithmic sophistication. Diminishing returns on a fixed catalog are real and arrive within weeks. Subsidies and content-development partnerships should be central to EdTech rollouts.
- **EdTech in low-resource environments.** Freedom's user base is precisely the population for whom EdTech has been promoted as a scalable solution: children with limited at-home access to age-appropriate English reading material, often in households where parents have limited English proficiency. The paper's positive engagement results — and especially the at-scale improvement — provide cleaner evidence for EdTech's potential than most prior literature, which often confounds personalization with UI changes (p. 5).
- **Personalization for moderate-scale platforms.** Most personalization evidence comes from massive-scale platforms (Netflix >200M, Spotify >500M users). The paper shows preference-based collaborative filtering can work effectively at ~6,400 DAU — a salient result for the many EdTech, public-sector, or non-profit platforms operating at moderate scale (p. 5-6).
- **Continuous evaluation as policy infrastructure.** The RCT-then-RDD design uses a mechanical eligibility threshold to maintain quasi-experimental variation indefinitely without needing a permanent holdout. This is a generalizable evaluation template for any algorithmic public-service deployment (job-matching, benefit-eligibility recommendation, learning content) and could be adopted by public-sector data offices (p. 7).
- **Preference vs. ability personalization.** Existing evidence (Muralidharan et al. 2019, Kumar & Mehra 2024) showed that *ability*-based content adaptation raises learning. This paper adds that *preference*-based recommendation also raises engagement and may raise learning indirectly through engagement. Policymakers can therefore consider both axes: matching content to skill *and* matching content to interest (p. 5).

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Engagement is not learning.** The most important caveat the authors flag: they measure engagement directly and infer learning from a literature linking the two (p. 4). For policy use cases, follow-up studies on test-score outcomes would be required.
- **Five-month horizon.** The content-saturation finding emerges by week 3-4; whether it dominates further at one to two years is unmeasured. Likewise, longer-term retention (does the user stay on the platform six months later?) is not the primary outcome.
- **Single-app, single-country.** Freedom is one app in India; collaborative filtering's data demands and content-saturation effect are general, but the magnitudes are not necessarily transferable.
- **Comparison with classic voltage-drop literature.** List (2022), Brandon et al. (2022), Vivalt (2020) document that interventions typically shrink at scale due to context dependence, implementation drift, and population representativeness (p. 6). The authors are careful to note their result reverses this for *algorithmic* interventions specifically — in their setting, algorithmic learning is the dominant force. They do not claim the result generalizes to non-algorithmic policies.
- **Tension with adaptive-learning evidence.** Muralidharan et al. (2019) report 0.37 SD learning gains from ability-based adaptation; Kumar & Mehra (2024) find 0.16 SD on standardized exams. The present paper does not measure SD-equivalents on test scores, so direct quantitative comparison is not possible.
- **Susan Athey's broader research agenda.** Susan Athey has been central to the literature on machine learning and causal inference (Athey & Imbens 2019). This paper extends that program to *deployment* — using ML for both targeting and ongoing causal estimation in the same product.
- **Implications for the algorithmic personalization debate in education.** Critics worry that algorithmic recommendations can narrow exposure (filter bubbles) or substitute for teacher judgment. The authors do not engage these concerns directly, but the result that the algorithm exhausts high-quality matches within weeks is *consistent* with concerns about content homogenization on small libraries.
