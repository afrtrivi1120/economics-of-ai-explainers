# Learning from Ricardo and Thompson: Machinery and Labor in the Early Industrial Revolution, and in the Age of AI

**Authors:** Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson
**Year:** 2024
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 32416
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/johnson_2024_ricardo-thompson-ai.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/johnson_2024_ricardo-thompson-ai.pdf)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w32416

## 1. Full citation

Acemoglu, Daron and Simon Johnson (2024). "Learning from Ricardo and Thompson: Machinery and Labor in the Early Industrial Revolution, and in the Age of AI." NBER Working Paper No. 32416, May 2024. (The repository metadata record lists only Johnson; the paper is co-authored with Acemoglu.)

## 2. Research question

Under what conditions does the introduction of new machinery — from the power loom to artificial intelligence — actually translate into higher wages and better living standards for workers? The paper uses the experience of British cotton weavers between 1780 and 1830 as a historical mirror to rethink the so-called "productivity bandwagon" in the age of AI.

## 3. Method

- **Historical data:** Series of nominal and real wages for British hand-loom weavers and factory operatives (1780–1862), drawn from Wood (1910), price indices from Allen (2007) and Clark (2005, 2007, 2010), parliamentary reports from 1835, and employment data from Bythell (1969), Chapman (1987), and Hammond and Hammond (1919) (pp. 7–13).
- **Strategy:** A structured economic-history case study organized around two figures: David Ricardo (his change of mind on machinery between 1817 and 1821) and E.P. Thompson (power, autonomy, and working conditions in *The Making of the English Working Class*, 1966) (pp. 1–4).
- **Analytical framework:** The distinction between average and marginal productivity of labor, automation vs. the creation of "new tasks," and the role of political institutions and collective bargaining in distributing productivity gains (pp. 2–3, notes 6–9).
- **Key assumptions:** Worker well-being depends on both technology and the balance of power; there is no automatic connection between aggregate productivity and wages.

## 4. Key findings

- Mechanization of spinning reduced the labor time needed to spin 100 pounds of cotton from 50,000 person-hours (early 18th century) to 2,000 hours after Crompton's mule (1780), 1,000 hours with the 100-spindle mule (1790), and only 300 hours with water-assisted mules (~1795) (p. 5).
- The cotton sector grew from ~1% of British GDP in the 1780s to 7–8% in 1811–1813 and accounted for 42.1% of exports by value in 1814–1816 (p. 8).
- Despite this boom, the nominal weekly wages of hand-loom weavers fell from 240 pence (1806) to 99 pence (1820), while factory operatives held steady near 120 pence (p. 9). In real terms, hand-loom weavers' wages collapsed to roughly 25% of their "golden age" level (1780–1800) (p. 10).
- Power looms generated little compensating employment: only ~10,000 workers in weaving factories in 1817 and 11,000 in 1820, against 240,000 hand-loom weavers still active in 1820 (pp. 8, 12). The sector's weighted average wage fell by roughly 50% between 1806 and 1820 (p. 13).
- Aggregate real wages in the British economy stagnated from the late 18th century through at least 1820 and possibly through the 1830s, when railways and new tasks widened labor demand (pp. 13–14).
- Fewer than 10% of the British adult population had the right to vote before 1832; the absence of political voice and collective bargaining rights was decisive in preventing productivity gains from being shared (p. 4, note 10).

## 5. Policy implications

The central lesson for policymakers is that accelerated AI adoption in sectors such as business services, banking, retail, health, or public services does not, by itself, guarantee better wages or higher-quality employment. The paper points to three policy levers:

1. **Technological direction:** Subsidies, public procurement, and R&D funding should favor AI applications that **complement** workers (creation of new tasks, capability augmentation) rather than pure substitution. This is especially relevant in economies with large informal sectors where displaced workers have limited safety nets.
- **Labor voice and collective bargaining:** The wage stagnation of hand-loom weavers was partly explained by the suppression of worker "combination" (p. 3). Strengthening sectoral unions, works councils, and social-dialogue mechanisms is an indispensable institutional complement if productivity gains are to flow through to wages.
- **Working conditions and algorithmic surveillance:** Acemoglu and Johnson warn (p. 18) that AI can be used for intensive monitoring and the degradation of worker autonomy. A forward-looking regulatory agenda should include limits on algorithmic monitoring, transparency requirements for AI-driven management systems, and labor data protections.

The historical case is an argument against policies that assume an automatic "trickle-down" from AI adoption to formal employment: the British economy took **at least four decades** to share the gains from cotton mechanization.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Vs. Aghion's growth optimism:** Aghion and Bunel (`aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth`) and Aghion-Jones-Jones (`aghion-jones-jones_2017_ai-economic-growth`) present AI as a new technological wave capable of raising GDP growth by 0.8–1.3 percentage points per year. Acemoglu and Johnson here — and Acemoglu in `acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`, where he estimates TFP gains of ~0.66% over 10 years — offer a far more cautious analogy: the Industrial Revolution confirmed productivity gains, but not wage gains (pp. 13–15).
- **Vs. Autor (`autor_2022_labor-market-impacts-tech`, `autor_2024_rebuild-middle-class`):** Autor emphasizes the possibility that AI could *complement* middle-skill workers. This paper shares that hope but conditions it on explicit policy choices, not on any inevitable technological outcome (p. 18).
- **Vs. revisionist views (Mokyr, Kelly et al. 2023):** Some economic historians (cited in notes 38, 41) argue that aggregate real wages were already growing in the 1810s. Acemoglu and Johnson acknowledge the debate but defend the Allen and Feinstein indices, according to which the break does not arrive until the 1830s (pp. 14–15).
- **Methodological caveat:** This is an economic-history essay, not a causal identification exercise. Inferences about AI rest on structural analogy, not statistical evidence.
- **Caveat on Ricardo:** The authors acknowledge that Ricardo changed his mind based on parliamentary observation of the power loom, not on a formal model (p. 2); his reversal is an empirical intuition, not a theorem.
