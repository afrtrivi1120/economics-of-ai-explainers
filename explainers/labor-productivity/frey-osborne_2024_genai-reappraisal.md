# Generative AI and the Future of Work: A Reappraisal

**Authors:** Carl Benedikt Frey, Michael Osborne
**Year:** 2024 (Oxford Martin Working Paper No. 2023; forthcoming in *Brown Journal of World Affairs*)
**Venue:** Brown Journal of World Affairs (Oxford Martin School WP)
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/frey-osborne_2024_genai-reappraisal.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/frey-osborne_2024_genai-reappraisal.pdf)
**Source:** https://oms-www.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/academic/2023-FoW-Working-Paper-Generative-AI-and-the-Future-of-Work-A-Reappraisal-combined.pdf

## 1. Full citation

Frey, C. B., & Osborne, M. A. (2024). *Generative AI and the Future of Work: A Reappraisal*. Oxford Martin Working Paper Series on the Future of Work, No. 2023. Forthcoming in *Brown Journal of World Affairs*.

This is the "rewrite" the same authors produced of their famous 2013 study ("The Future of Employment"), the one that estimated that **47% of jobs in the U.S. were exposed to automation** (p. 1, footnote 4) — a figure that dominated the global labor policy debate for more than a decade, including much of the global conversation about the "fourth industrial revolution."

## 2. Research question

Does the arrival of generative AI (LLMs such as GPT-4, image generators such as DALL-E 2) change the conclusions of their 2013 study about the "human refuges" from automation — that is, the tasks requiring creativity, social intelligence, and unstructured manual work (p. 1)?

## 3. Method

Unlike the 2013 paper (which was a quantitative exercise based on O*NET occupational classification with an expert panel), this is a **qualitative and narrative reappraisal**, not econometric. The authors:

- Review the technical progress of the past decade in LLMs, computer vision, generative adversarial networks (GANs), and autonomous vehicles (pp. 2–7).
- Re-examine the three "bottlenecks" that in 2013 shielded human employment: social intelligence, creativity, and physical perception/manipulation (Moravec's paradox) (pp. 3–8).
- Cite recent experimental evidence on AI-assisted productivity — GitHub Copilot (+56% speed), ChatGPT on writing tasks, customer-service agents (+14%) (p. 9, notes 31–33).
- **Key assumption:** the economic productivity of AI is constrained by data bottlenecks (diminishing returns to scaling), reliability in high-stakes environments, and the political economy of labor resistance (pp. 4, 10).

## 4. Key findings

- **The "47%" holds directionally, but the contours shift.** Generative AI extended the potential reach of automation into virtual social and creative tasks that in 2013 were considered safe — journalists, designers, advertising executives, programmers (pp. 1–2, 8).
- **In-person interaction is the new trench.** "LLMs don't have bodies": the art of face-to-face persuasion will remain valuable in managerial, medical, and relational-sales occupations (p. 3).
- **AI creativity is recombinatorial, not conceptual.** The models produce "sequels, not new narratives" — they can write another Batman script but not *The Seventh Seal*; they remix Mozart but do not produce Arvo Pärt (p. 8).
- **The most likely short-run labor effect is not displacement but wage compression.** The analogy is Uber: when GPS eliminated street knowledge as a barrier, taxi drivers' hourly earnings fell 10% when Uber entered a city (p. 9, note 30). LLMs would do the same to writers, designers, and programmers: more entrants, more competition, downward wage pressure on incumbents (pp. 9–10).
- **Novices and lower-skill workers gain disproportionately** from AI assistance (Copilot, ChatGPT, support agents: +14%) (p. 9, note 33).
- **The technical frontier is approaching diminishing returns:** training data has already consumed much of the web; synthetic data can cause "model collapse"; RLHF showed signs of saturation; hallucination rates increased from 18% (GPT-3) to 33–34% (GPT-4) on human-like fallacious judgments (pp. 4, 10–11, note 40).
- **White-collar political resistance.** Unlike the factory automation of the 20th century, those affected now (Hollywood screenwriters, journalists) have organizing capacity and political weight — the WGA/SAG-AFTRA strike is the case in point (p. 10).

## 5. Policy implications

1. **Re-read the "47%" with caution.** Studies derived from the original 2013 paper estimated exposure rates as high as 60–70% for many developing economies (IDB, ILO). Frey and Osborne's reappraisal suggests that the real short-run risk is not mass unemployment but **wage compression among urban white-collar workers** — junior programmers, journalists, designers, call-center agents. In economies with large business-process outsourcing sectors, the Uber-in-LLM effect is directly relevant.
2. **Complementarity policy before protection policy.** If novices gain more from AI, there is a strong case for subsidizing access to generative AI tools in vocational training systems, public universities, and small and medium enterprises — AI can act as a "skill equalizer" (consistent with `brynjolfsson-li-raymond_2025_genai-at-work`).
3. **Prioritize in-person interaction skills.** Technical and secondary education should reinforce socioemotional competencies, relationship management, and unstructured physical trades (construction, health, care) — the "refuges" that survive the reappraisal.
4. **Anticipate distributional conflict, not just unemployment.** The paper suggests that the real political-economic risk is friction between incumbents (with voice) and entrants (without it). In many developing economies, where formal unionism is weak but informality is high, this translates into pressure on mid-level formal wages.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Against the macro-optimism of Aghion:** Frey and Osborne sit firmly in the skeptical camp. Their emphasis on bottlenecks (data, reliability, physical embodiment, hallucinations) resonates with the conservative view of `acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai` — a modest TFP contribution, not a boom — and contrasts with the 0.8–1.3 pp/year additional growth projection of `aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth` or the "growth explosion" scenario of `aghion-jones-jones_2017_ai-economic-growth` and `jones_2026_ai-economic-future`.
- **Self-revision without a new metric.** The paper does *not* recalculate the 47%. It is an interpretive essay, not a replicable quantitative exercise. Readers expecting a new probability table by occupation will be unsatisfied — that accounting remains that of the 2013 paper.
- **Internal tension on creativity.** The authors concede that generative AI has invaded a "refuge" they had declared safe in 2013 (creativity, pp. 5–6) but insist that conceptual creativity remains human. The line between "recombination" and "creation" is not operationalized.
- **Ricardo-Thompson lens.** The discussion of white-collar distributional conflict (p. 10) speaks directly to `johnson_2024_ricardo-thompson-ai`: the direction of technical change is not exogenous; it depends on who has voice. Frey and Osborne are less explicit than Johnson about the role of the state in *directing* that trajectory.
- **On "complementarity for novices":** the evidence they cite (Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond, Noy-Zhang, Peng et al.) is short-run and comes from controlled settings. If AI turns novices into perfect substitutes for experts, the equilibrium outcome may be generalized wage decline, not upward leveling (see parallel discussion in `autor_2024_rebuild-middle-class`).
