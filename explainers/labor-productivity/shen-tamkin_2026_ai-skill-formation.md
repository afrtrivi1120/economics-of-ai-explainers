# How AI Impacts Skill Formation

**Authors:** Judy Hanwen Shen, Alex Tamkin (Anthropic)
**Year:** 2026 (January 29, 2026)
**Venue:** arXiv preprint 2601.20245 — work done as part of the Anthropic Fellows Program
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/shen-tamkin_2026_ai-skill-formation.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/shen-tamkin_2026_ai-skill-formation.pdf)
**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.20245

## 1. Full citation

Shen, J. H., & Tamkin, A. (2026). *How AI Impacts Skill Formation*. arXiv:2601.20245v1. Anthropic Fellows Program.

## 2. Research question

Most of the existing literature on AI in the workplace measures the *product* of AI assistance — lines of code written, tickets resolved, tasks completed. Shen and Tamkin instead ask about the *process*. When a worker uses AI to complete a task that requires a new skill, do they actually acquire that skill, or do they just produce the deliverable? Concretely, the paper poses two pre-registered research questions: (RQ1) Does AI assistance improve task completion productivity when new skills are required? (RQ2) How does using AI assistance affect the development of those new skills? (p. 4-5). The motivation is that real workplaces will increasingly require humans to *supervise* AI-generated outputs, especially in safety-critical settings, and that supervisory competence is impossible without underlying skill formation (p. 1-2).

## 3. Method

- **Setting.** A randomized between-subjects online experiment with 52 professional or freelance software developers recruited through a third-party crowd-worker platform. Compensation was a flat 150 USD, designed to reduce the threat of stereotype effects in self-reported demographics (p. 7-9).
- **Task.** Two coding tasks using the Python *Trio* library — an asynchronous concurrency library deliberately chosen because (i) it is *less* well-known than `asyncio`, (ii) it introduces concepts (structured concurrency, nurseries, memory channels, error handling for async functions) that are genuinely new to most participants, and (iii) it is documented well enough that learning is feasible inside the 35-minute task window (p. 5-6). Recruitment criteria required >1 year of Python experience, weekly Python use, prior AI assistant experience, and *no* prior Trio exposure (p. 7).
- **Treatment.** Random assignment to (a) an AI-assisted condition with a chat-based GPT-4o assistant integrated into the coding interface and primed to act as an "intelligent coding assistant" with full visibility into participant code, or (b) a no-AI control with the same starter code and library tutorial but no assistant (p. 6, Figure 4).
- **Outcome measures.** (i) Task completion time and a binary completion indicator. (ii) A 14-question, 27-point post-task quiz administered without AI access in both conditions, covering Trio concepts via three question types: *Conceptual* understanding, *Code Reading*, and *Debugging*. Code-writing questions were excluded to avoid confounding with syntax errors easily fixed by AI or web search (p. 6). The quiz was developed using item response theory to ensure independence between questions and absence of local item dependence (p. 6).
- **Effect size and power.** A pilot study (n=20) yielded a Cohen's d=1.7 quiz-score effect; the main study was pre-registered for d=0.85 to guard against effect-size inflation typical in pilots (p. 9, footnote 2 with OSF link).
- **Qualitative analysis.** All 51 screen recordings (one missing in the AI condition) were manually annotated for AI-interaction events, AI queries, errors encountered, and active coding time, allowing the authors to construct a six-cluster typology of human-AI interaction patterns (p. 12).

## 4. Key findings

- **No statistically significant productivity gain.** AI access did not significantly change task completion time in the main study (mean 23 vs. 25 minutes, p=0.391; Figure 6, p. 9). However, every AI participant completed task 2 within the time limit, while 4 of 26 control participants did not (p. 14).
- **Significant skill-formation loss.** AI participants scored about **17 percentage points (2 grade points)** lower on the post-task quiz (Cohen's d=0.738, p=0.010; controlling for warm-up time the effect remains at d=0.725, p=0.016; p. 9). The pilot effect (d=1.7) was even larger, suggesting the main study is a conservative estimate (p. 9).
- **Loss is concentrated in debugging skill.** Decomposing the quiz by question type, the largest score gap is on debugging questions, the smallest on code-reading questions (Figure 8, p. 11). The AI group encountered roughly **one-third the number of errors** (median 1 vs. 3, p. 17) — and errors that *force* a learner to grapple with Trio-specific concepts (`TypeError`, `RuntimeWarning` for un-awaited coroutines) appear in the control condition but largely vanish in the AI condition (Figure 15, p. 17).
- **Less active coding time.** AI participants spent much less time *actively* writing code; their time shifted to composing queries (some up to 11 minutes total) and reading AI output. Within each condition, more active coding time correlated with higher quiz scores (Figure 16, p. 18).
- **Six interaction patterns, sharply bimodal in learning.** Three high-scoring patterns (quiz score 65-86%) and three low-scoring patterns (24-39%) emerge (Figure 11, p. 13). High-scoring patterns — *Generation-Then-Comprehension*, *Hybrid Code-Explanation*, *Conceptual Inquiry* — all involve cognitive engagement: generating code and then asking the AI to explain it, mixing code generation with explanation requests, or asking *only* conceptual questions and resolving errors independently. Low-scoring patterns — *AI Delegation* (paste-the-prompt, paste-the-answer), *Progressive AI Reliance*, *Iterative AI Debugging* — involve cognitive offloading.
- **AI users found the task easier and reported lower learning.** Self-reported task difficulty was lower in the AI condition, but self-reported learning was also lower (~4.1 vs. ~4.9 on a 7-point scale) and feedback included spontaneous comments about feeling "lazy" and recognizing "lots of gaps" in their own understanding (p. 18, Figure 9).

## 5. Policy implications

- **AI productivity numbers are not free.** Most workplace AI studies (Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond 2025, Cui et al. 2024, Peng et al. 2023) measure short-run output, not learning. This paper provides the first randomized evidence that for tasks requiring *new* skills, the productivity calculus must be netted against the cost of impaired skill acquisition. Policymakers and firms evaluating return on AI deployment should commission longitudinal skill-retention studies, not just task-completion benchmarks.
- **Workforce training programs must rethink AI integration.** Junior-developer onboarding, vocational coding bootcamps, and reskilling programs that bake AI tools into the curriculum risk producing workers who pass tasks but cannot supervise the AI's output. The paper's interaction-pattern typology suggests a concrete intervention: instructional designs that *require* explanation queries or conceptual questions (and discourage paste-and-go workflows) may preserve learning. Major LLM services already ship "study modes" (ChatGPT Study Mode, Claude Code Learning/Explanatory mode), and the evidence in this paper supports their use as a default for novice workers (p. 19).
- **Safety-critical and high-stakes domains need explicit policy.** The authors flag that AI-written code in regulated environments — medical devices, financial systems, transportation — must still be debugged and verified by humans (p. 1). If junior engineers' debugging skill is eroded by AI assistance, organizations face an *aging-out* risk: the senior engineers who can supervise AI today may not be replaced. Regulators should consider competence requirements (e.g., mandatory unaided code-review certification) in domains where AI is permitted as a productivity tool.
- **Compensation and incentives.** Workers paid by output but tasked with absorbing new tools have an incentive to delegate maximally to AI. Employers who care about long-run capability should reward demonstrated understanding (e.g., periodic skill audits) and not only completion rates.
- **Agentic systems will amplify the problem.** The chat interface in this study still requires the human to type a query and read a response — a non-trivial cognitive load. Fully agentic coding tools, where the human just describes intent, will likely produce *larger* skill-formation losses (p. 19). The window in which to design human-in-the-loop workflows that preserve learning is closing.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Tension with productivity-uplift studies.** Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond (2025) (`brynjolfsson-li-raymond_2025_genai-at-work`) and Cui et al. (2024) report 14-26% productivity gains from generative AI in customer service and software development, with the largest gains concentrated among novices. Shen-Tamkin do not contradict those measurements — their participants did complete tasks slightly faster on average — but they show that for *novel* skills, average task time is the wrong yardstick. The two literatures study different time horizons: same-task productivity vs. cross-task transfer.
- **Sample size and external validity.** Only 52 participants in the main study, all crowd-recruited freelance or professional Python developers, completing a 35-minute task with no real-world incentive to learn (p. 19). The authors flag this as a "lower bound" since chat-based interfaces require more cognitive engagement than agentic coding tools (p. 19).
- **One library, one language, one hour.** Skill formation in real workplaces unfolds over months. Generalizing to longer time horizons requires longitudinal field studies the authors explicitly call for (p. 19).
- **Compatibility with Acemoglu's macro view.** Acemoglu (2024) (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`) argues for a conservative ~0.66% TFP gain over 10 years, partly on the grounds that AI's task-level gains may not aggregate cleanly. Shen-Tamkin provide a micro-mechanism that supports caution: if AI gains come at the cost of skill depreciation, the aggregate productivity profile may be front-loaded and then plateau or reverse as workforce competence erodes.
- **Inverted from the "skill leveler" view.** Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond and Cruces et al. (2026) (`cruces-et-al_2026_genai-education-productivity-gaps`) report that AI compresses productivity gaps in favor of less-skilled workers. Shen-Tamkin show that this leveling, when it occurs in tasks involving *new* skills, may be partially illusory — leveling task output without leveling underlying competence.
- **Cognitive offloading literature.** Aligns with Lee et al. (2025) and Gerlich (2025) on reduced critical thinking with AI use, but the present paper offers the cleanest randomized evidence that the offloading is causal and behaviorally specific to AI delegation patterns.
