# The Labor Market Impacts of Technological Change: From Unbridled Enthusiasm to Qualified Optimism to Vast Uncertainty

**Authors:** David Autor
**Year:** 2022
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 30074 (May 2022, Revised July 2022)
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/autor_2022_labor-market-impacts-tech.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/autor_2022_labor-market-impacts-tech.pdf)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w30074

## 1. Full citation

Autor, David (2022). "The Labor Market Impacts of Technological Change: From Unbridled Enthusiasm to Qualified Optimism to Vast Uncertainty." NBER Working Paper No. 30074. Cambridge, MA: National Bureau of Economic Research.

## 2. Research question

How has economic thinking evolved, over four decades, about the relationship between digital technological change and wage inequality? And how well equipped are those analytical frameworks to understand the labor-market effects of artificial intelligence? Autor reviews four successive paradigms — the education race, the task-polarization model, the automation-reinstatement race, and the era of AI uncertainty — to argue that traditional optimism about technology has given way to genuine uncertainty (p. 1).

## 3. Method

- **Paper type:** conceptual review essay prepared for the Brookings Global Forum on Democracy and Technology, not a new empirical study. It synthesizes four generations of literature on technology and wage inequality.
- **Secondary data reproduced:** college/high-school wage premiums 1963–2012 (Figure 1, based on Autor 2014), real earnings changes by education level 1963–2017 (Figure 2, based on Autor 2019), occupational polarization in the U.S. 1970–2016 (Figure 3) and EU 1993–2010 (Figure 4, Goos et al. 2014), demographic-group exposure to routine-task displacement (Figure 5, Acemoglu and Restrepo 2021), and evidence on AI job postings (Figure 7, Acemoglu et al. 2022a).
- **Analytical framework:** starts from the premise that wages depend on productivity and the relative scarcity of skills, then traces how four successive models formalize the interaction between technology and relative labor demand (p. 1).
- **Key assumptions:** digital technology reduces the cost of symbolic processing (Nordhaus 2007), which reallocates tasks between workers and machines; "Polanyi's paradox" historically delimited what machines could do (p. 8); with AI that constraint weakens ("Polanyi's revenge," p. 19).

## 4. Key findings

- Under the "education race" model (Tinbergen–Goldin–Katz), growth in education-based earnings gaps explains roughly **60% of the rise in income inequality between 1980 and 2017, and 40% between 2000 and 2017** in the U.S. (p. 3).
- Between 1979 and 2017, weekly real earnings for men with a postgraduate degree rose 43% and for those with a bachelor's degree 12%, while they **fell 10% (some college), 21% (high school), and 25% (no high school diploma)**; this collapse at the bottom is inconsistent with a pure education-complementarity model, given that the supply of workers without a college degree actually contracted (p. 5, note 7: the share of hours worked by high-school-or-less workers fell from >75% in 1963 to <40% in 2017).
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2021), as cited in this review, estimate that **50%–70% of the rise in between-group wage inequality from 1980 to 2016 — and all of the fall in real wages for men without a high school diploma — is attributable to the adverse effects of automation** on groups that were initially concentrated in routine tasks (p. 13).
- More than **60% of U.S. employment in 2018 was in occupational titles that did not exist in 1940** (Figure 6, p. 17), which is the central evidence for the "task reinstatement" paradigm. New job creation has polarized: for non-college workers it shifted from middle-wage occupations (1940–1980) to low-wage personal services (1980–2018), while for college workers it concentrated in professional and managerial occupations (p. 17).
- Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019), cited here, find that **automation and reinstatement were in balance during 1950–1987, but automation outpaced reinstatement in 1987–2017**, consistent with the simultaneous fall in labor's share of national income (p. 15).
- In the AI era, Acemoglu et al. (2022a) show that establishments with task structures more suited to AI in 2010 differentially increased their AI job postings between 2010 and 2018 (coefficient 9.19, SE 1.21, Figure 7, p. 21); but aggregate effects on occupations and industries are not yet detectable (p. 22).
- Despite large educational upgrading (men with a BA or more at the earnings median rose from 20% in 1979 to 36% in 2019, a 75% increase), **real earnings for the median male worker rose only 10% over four decades** (p. 27).

## 5. Policy implications (Colombia / Latin America)

- **The occupational-polarization model does not transfer mechanically to developing economies.** Autor notes this explicitly: "Polarization does not, however, describe the experience of developing countries, where the skill levels associated with different tasks are quite different and computerization is less pervasive and still relatively expensive in comparison with human labor (Maloney and Molina, 2016)" (p. 10, note 10). For Colombia, this means that the simple prescription of "automate routine tasks" takes a different shape: the relative cost of labor makes capital-labor substitution less attractive in many sectors, but asymmetric AI diffusion may leapfrog stages and hit middle-level cognitive tasks directly (analysts, advisors, accounting).
- **Education supply is necessary but not sufficient.** The U.S. case (75% more college-educated workers at the median, yet only 10% more real wages over four decades, p. 27) warns Colombia that expanding tertiary coverage without parallel institutional reforms — realistic minimum wages, formalization, collective bargaining, portable benefits — does not guarantee that productivity gains translate into higher wages.
- **Reinstatement is not automatic.** If the creation of "new work" polarizes toward poorly paid personal services (p. 17), the net effect for Colombia could be a growth of low-quality informal employment. Industrial policy and directed innovation (R&D that complements, rather than substitutes, the skills of the local workforce) deserve a central, not marginal, place.
- **"Polanyi's revenge" demands AI auditing.** When "computers now know more than they can tell us" (p. 19), algorithmic decisions in credit, education, subsidy targeting, or justice require explainable-AI frameworks and public oversight. This is an active regulatory agenda, not a technical task that can be delegated away.
- **Monitor labor's share of income, not just the unemployment rate.** Autor stresses that income inequality (wage and non-wage) can surge even when employment remains stable (p. 23). For Latin America, which already has high capital concentration, this channel is of first-order importance.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Autor vs. more radical views on total displacement.** Against Susskind (2020), Autor rejects the image of a "future without jobs" and argues that new-work creation is endogenous and persistent (p. 13, p. 26). This contrasts with more alarmist narratives in parts of the AGI discussion; see `korinek-vipra_2025_concentrating-intelligence` and `jones_2026_ai-economic-future` for more disruptive transition scenarios.
- **Ambiguity about macro magnitudes.** Autor is cautious and does not offer his own point estimates of AI's aggregate impact on TFP. This places him between the conservatism of `acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai` (~0.66% TFP gain over 10 years) and the more optimistic forecasts of `aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth` (0.8–1.3 pp/year). Autor himself acknowledges "vast uncertainty" (title and p. 28) about the trajectory.
- **Polarization: universal stylized fact or Anglo-Saxon sample bias?** Figures 3 and 4 document polarization in the U.S. and EU, but the evidence for Latin America is weaker. Autor acknowledges this in a footnote (p. 10, note 10), though the implication remains underdeveloped in the body of the paper.
- **The task framework may be insufficient for AI.** Autor says so explicitly: "while the task framework offers a useful starting point... it is unlikely to be encompassing enough" (p. 20). Alternative lenses: Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb (AI as a prediction machine) and Korinek-Stiglitz, cited at p. 20; see `agrawal-gans-goldfarb_2025_research-agenda-tai` and `korinek_2025_ai-agents-econ-research`.
- **Causal attribution of the fall in labor's share.** Autor accepts the Acemoglu and Restrepo results, but acknowledges that non-technological factors — de-unionization, declining real minimum wages, globalization — are co-determinants (p. 6).
