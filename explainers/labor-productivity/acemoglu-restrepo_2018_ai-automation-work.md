# Artificial Intelligence, Automation and Work

**Authors:** Daron Acemoglu, Pascual Restrepo
**Year:** 2018
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 24196 (prepared for *Economics of Artificial Intelligence*, eds. Agrawal, Goldfarb, and Gans)
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/acemoglu-restrepo_2018_ai-automation-work.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/acemoglu-restrepo_2018_ai-automation-work.pdf)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w24196

## 1. Full citation

Acemoglu, Daron and Pascual Restrepo (2018). "Artificial Intelligence, Automation and Work." NBER Working Paper No. 24196, January 2018. JEL J23, J24.

## 2. Research question

How does automation in general — and AI and robotics in particular — affect labor demand, wages, employment, and labor's share of national income? The essay aims to move beyond the "false dichotomy" between alarmism ("AI will destroy all jobs") and neoclassical optimism ("technology always raises labor demand") by proposing a unified conceptual framework grounded in *tasks* (p. 1).

## 3. Method

- **Type of work:** conceptual essay / theoretical survey synthesizing Acemoglu–Restrepo (2016) and Zeira (1998), drawing on Acemoglu–Autor (2011) (p. 1).
- **Analytical framework:** a task-based approach rather than the canonical production function F(AL, BK) with factor-augmenting technical change (p. 5). The minimum unit of production is the task; labor and capital have distinct comparative advantages across different tasks.
- **Operational definitions:**
  - *Automation (extensive margin):* expansion of the set of tasks that can be produced with capital (p. 5).
  - *Deepening of automation (intensive margin):* productivity improvements in tasks that are already automated (p. 2, p. 8).
  - *Creation of new tasks:* functions where labor retains a comparative advantage (p. 9).
- **Key assumptions:** technical change does not necessarily increase labor demand; labor supply may be quasi-elastic due to labor-market imperfections (p. 6); skill heterogeneity exists.
- **Empirical evidence cited (not original to this paper):** historical examples (looms, the cotton gin, agricultural mechanization, ATMs, industrial robots) and statistics drawn from Syverson (2017), Gordon (2016), and Acemoglu–Restrepo (2016, 2017).

## 4. Key findings

- **Displacement effect:** automation replaces labor in tasks previously performed by humans, reducing labor demand, wages, and labor's share of national income (p. 1, pp. 5–6). This effect is absent from canonical factor-augmenting models (p. 5).
- **Four countervailing forces** (pp. 6–9):
  1. *Productivity effect:* cheaper automated tasks increase labor demand in non-automated tasks (e.g., ATMs and bank tellers, Bessen 2016, p. 6).
  2. *Capital accumulation:* higher demand for capital induces investment that raises labor demand (p. 7).
  3. *Deepening of automation:* intensive-margin improvements generate productivity gains without additional displacement (p. 8).
  4. *Creation of new tasks (reinstatement effect):* the most powerful force for sustaining labor's share of income (p. 10).
- **Historical magnitude of new tasks:** "from 1980 to 2010, the introduction and expansion of new tasks and job titles explains about half of employment growth" (citing Acemoglu–Restrepo 2016, p. 9).
- **"So-so technologies":** the real danger to labor comes not from highly productive breakthrough technologies but from technologies that are "just productive enough" to be adopted and displace workers, yet not productive enough to generate large productivity effects (p. 7).
- **Disappointing U.S. productivity:** labor productivity growth of 2.7% (1947–1973) fell to 1.5% (1974–1994), rebounded to 2.8% (1995–2004), and declined again to 1.3% (2005–2015) (citing Syverson 2017, p. 14). The framework attributes this to possible *excessive automation* (tax-code biases favoring capital, labor-market frictions) and a slowdown in the creation of new tasks (pp. 13–14).
- **Skills mismatch:** new tasks require new skills; when the education system fails to provide them, adjustment slows and productivity gains shrink (p. 13).

## 5. Policy implications

The displacement–reinstatement framework is directly applicable to AI policy design:

- **Tax policy:** Acemoglu–Restrepo argue that the U.S. tax code subsidizes capital over labor (accelerated depreciation, interest deductions) and pushes toward excessive automation (p. 14). Policymakers should audit whether their depreciation rules, investment deductions, and non-wage labor costs create an analogous bias — a concern particularly acute in economies with high informality and low aggregate productivity.
- **Education and skills mismatch:** the creation of AI-linked new tasks only translates into employment when complementary skills exist (p. 13). This reinforces the urgency of technical training and retraining programs — through vocational training systems and micro-credential programs — aligned with the tasks that AI *complements*: judgment, communication, and ethical oversight of systems.
- **Watch out for "so-so technologies":** low-value customer-service chatbots and administrative automation in the public sector and services can displace formal employment without generating the productivity gains that would finance the transition (p. 7). Industrial policy should prioritize AI adoption in tasks with high productivity effects (health, personalized education, logistics) rather than superficial automation.
- **Policy for new-task creation:** the paper argues this does not happen automatically (p. 11) — it depends on incentives, R&D, and the education system. This provides theoretical grounding for investing in domestic innovation capacity rather than simply importing automation.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Magnitude of AI's productivity effect:** Acemoglu–Restrepo (2018) are agnostic about the aggregate magnitude. In `acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`, Acemoglu quantifies the effect and finds a modest TFP gain (~0.66% cumulative over 10 years). Aghion and Bunel in `aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth` project 0.8–1.3 percentage points per year — a substantially more optimistic view. The 2018 task framework is compatible with both results, depending on how many tasks are automated and how elastic the supply of new tasks is.
- **Endogenous growth:** `aghion-jones-jones_2017_ai-economic-growth` models AI as a technology that automates the production of ideas, opening the door to explosive growth. Acemoglu–Restrepo are skeptical: they emphasize that without endogenous creation of new tasks the growth path is unbalanced (p. 9), and that current AI ("applied AI," pp. 11–12) is limited in scope, far from the general intelligence that justifies the Aghion–Jones–Jones models.
- **Micro-evidence on complementarity:** `brynjolfsson-li-raymond_2025_genai-at-work` documents average productivity gains of 14% for customer-service agents using generative AI, concentrated among less-experienced workers. This nuances the pure displacement narrative of the 2018 framework: in some contexts AI acts as capital *complementary* to less-skilled labor, not as a substitute.
- **Middle class and reinstatement:** `autor_2024_rebuild-middle-class` extends the "new tasks" argument into an explicit agenda for rebuilding middle-income employment; Acemoglu–Restrepo provide the theoretical apparatus, Autor the policy program.
- **Concentration of power:** `korinek-vipra_2025_concentrating-intelligence` warns that oligopolistic control of compute and models can bias the direction of technical change toward automation (rather than task creation). Acemoglu–Restrepo (2018) partially anticipate this by discussing "excessive automation," but they do not model market power among AI providers.
- **Methodological caveat:** the paper is an essay, not an empirical study. The figures cited come from other works. The validity of the framework depends on subsequent evidence (Acemoglu–Restrepo 2020 on robots, etc.).
