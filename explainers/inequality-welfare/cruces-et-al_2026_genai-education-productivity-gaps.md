# Does Generative AI Narrow Education-Based Productivity Gaps?

**Authors:** Guillermo Cruces, Diego Fernández Meijide, Sebastian Galiani, Ramiro H. Gálvez, María Lombardi
**Year:** 2026 (February 2026)
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 34851
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/cruces-et-al_2026_genai-education-productivity-gaps.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/cruces-et-al_2026_genai-education-productivity-gaps.pdf)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w34851

## 1. Full citation

Cruces, G., Fernández Meijide, D., Galiani, S., Gálvez, R. H., & Lombardi, M. (2026). *Does Generative AI Narrow Education-Based Productivity Gaps? Evidence from a Randomized Experiment*. NBER Working Paper No. 34851. National Bureau of Economic Research.

## 2. Research question

A central question in the inequality-and-AI debate is whether generative AI is *skill-biased* — complementing the already-skilled and widening gaps — or *skill-democratizing* — letting workers with less formal training perform tasks that previously required expertise. Existing field evidence comes mostly from inside firms or specific occupations (call centers, consulting, software development) where hiring and task assignment compress educational heterogeneity. As a result, the literature has documented compression *within* relatively homogeneous worker populations but said little about gaps *across* the broader education distribution. The paper asks: when individuals with sharply different levels of formal education perform the *same* general workplace task with or without AI assistance, does AI compress, eliminate, or preserve the productivity gap between them? (p. 1-2, 4-5).

## 3. Method

- **Sample.** A pre-registered (AEA RCT Registry 0016607) online randomized experiment conducted in Argentina between September and November 2025, with **1,174 adults aged 25-45** drawn from three reputable survey panels. Education classification is binary: low education = high-school diploma plus less than half of a postsecondary degree; high education = more than half completed (p. 6-7). Final sample is 520 low- and 654 high-education participants (p. 11).
- **Task.** A self-contained business problem-solving task simulating an email from the participant's boss describing a workplace scenario (cafeteria, food-delivery service, or home-appliances store) requiring the analysis of text, a figure, and a table. Participants must reply diagnosing the problem and proposing a solution. The task taps reading and data comprehension, reasoning, creative problem-solving, and writing, and was extensively piloted to be challenging-but-feasible without AI for low-education participants (p. 2, 7-8).
- **Treatment.** Random assignment to (a) treatment with access to a GPT-4.1-based AI assistant integrated into the experiment platform, preceded by a brief tutorial; or (b) control with no AI access. **84% of treated participants used the assistant**; control compliance was monitored by tracking whether participants left the page and by automated detection of AI-style writing (only ~13% of controls were classified as non-compliant; p. 11-12).
- **Compensation.** Up to AR$25,000 (~US$18) in supermarket gift cards; payment tier was tied to relative performance within own education-treatment cell to avoid distortions (p. 6, footnote 5).
- **Outcomes.** Pre-registered overall task score (0-10) is a weighted average of *content* (6 points: diagnosis + solution) and *writing* (4 points: spelling, grammar, clarity, organization, tone). Standardized relative to the low-education control mean. Grading was performed by GPT-5-mini against task-specific rubrics, run ten times and averaged; validated against (i) Claude Haiku 4.5 and Gemini Flash 3.0, (ii) an independent researcher's grading sets, (iii) two human graders on a 10% random sample (p. 9-10).
- **Identification.** OLS estimation of `Yi = β0 + β1·HighEducation + β2·Treatment + β3·HighEducation×Treatment + ui`, with β3 capturing the differential AI effect across education groups (p. 12). Heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. Pre-registration covers thresholds, primary outcomes, and analytical specifications.
- **Follow-up module.** All participants — including treated — completed an *unassisted* follow-up: an open-ended question about the root cause framed as a brief interaction with the boss, plus two multiple-choice questions on the figure and factual recall. This isolates whether AI access during the main task improves *retained* understanding (p. 2-3).

## 4. Key findings

- **Baseline education gap.** Without AI, high-education participants outperform low-education participants by **0.548 standard deviations** on the overall score (p. 13). This is the gap AI may or may not close.
- **AI raises both groups, but unequally.** Access to AI raises the score by **1.242 SD** for low-education participants and **0.834 SD** for high-education participants — both highly significant (p<0.01). The gap in treatment effects across education groups is **0.408 SD** (p<0.01) (p. 13).
- **AI closes ~75% of the baseline gap.** With AI access, the high-low gap falls from 0.548 SD to **0.139 SD** (p-value 0.110), so AI closes about three-quarters of the initial productivity gap (p. 2, 13). Low-education participants with AI substantially *outperform* (non-AI) high-education participants — a more-than-doubling of low-education output (1.242 SD vs. 0.548 SD; p. 13, footnote 18).
- **Underlying skills still matter.** In the unassisted follow-up, AI access during the main task produces only modest gains for low-education participants and no detectable gains for high-education participants. The follow-up education gap narrows only slightly and remains sizable, indicating that AI compresses *task execution* but not the underlying human-capital gap (p. 2-3).
- **Mechanism.** The authors interpret AI as a *task-level equalizer* that substitutes for cognitive inputs (abstract reasoning, written communication) more scarce among low-education participants. Differences in *how* the AI is used persist — high-education users prompt in more structured and disciplined ways — which plausibly explains the residual 0.139 SD gap with AI access (p. 2-3). They explicitly distinguish their finding from a "simplification" hypothesis (Althoff & Reichardt 2026) and from the broader skill-biased-technological-change literature: AI does not eliminate the role of human capital, it merely binds a *different* margin (p. 3-4, footnote 2).
- **Distributional shape.** Examining the full score distribution (not just means) confirms that with AI, low-education performance shifts substantially rightward, while high-education performance also rises but compresses against a ceiling (p. 13).

## 5. Policy implications

- **First direct evidence on between-group equalization.** Most prior workplace evidence — Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2025), Dell'Acqua et al. (2023), Cui et al. (Forthcoming), Noy & Zhang (2023) — finds AI compresses gaps *within* relatively homogeneous worker pools (p. 4). Cruces et al. provide the cleanest experimental evidence yet that AI can substantially compress *across* education groups, the dimension most relevant to inequality debates.
- **Adult education and labor-market re-entry.** For policymakers running adult-education, employability, or upskilling programs, the result implies that targeted AI access can be a low-cost way to lift the productivity of workers with limited formal credentials in general business problem-solving tasks — exactly the kind of work that has historically commanded a college wage premium.
- **Caution about over-interpreting average effects.** Policymakers should not conclude that AI eliminates the value of education. The follow-up evidence shows the gap reopens once the assistant is removed (p. 2-3). Tasks that require independent recall, supervision of AI output, or synthesis without access to AI will continue to favor more educated workers.
- **Distributional ambiguity at the labor-market level.** Whether task-level skill leveling translates into wage compression depends on (i) firm-level adoption decisions, (ii) whether AI-augmented low-education workers replace high-education workers or expand the labor force, and (iii) general-equilibrium adjustments to wages and prices (p. 3-4). The authors explicitly note these forces are beyond the scope of their experiment.
- **Implications for public-sector productivity.** Government services and routine knowledge work — drafting reports, analyzing simple data, writing constituent communications — match the skill profile of the experimental task. Expanding AI access among public employees with heterogeneous educational backgrounds is a plausible productivity-and-equity lever.
- **AI grading.** A methodological side benefit: the paper's careful validation of LLM rubric-based grading (cross-model, independent-grader, human spot-check) gives a practical template for low-cost large-scale evaluation in education research and policy program audits (p. 9-10).

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Tension with skill-formation evidence.** Shen & Tamkin (2026) (`shen-tamkin_2026_ai-skill-formation`) show that AI assistance impairs *skill acquisition* in coding tasks. Cruces et al. show that AI compresses *task output* across education groups but with limited persistence in unassisted follow-up. The two papers are complementary: AI levels execution today, but the underlying human-capital gap persists, and dependence on AI may slow human-capital accumulation. Long-run welfare depends on whether the leveling persists in equilibrium or fades when AI is unavailable.
- **Counter-evidence from heterogeneous samples.** Daniotti et al. (2026) find productivity gains concentrated among *experienced* developers; Otis et al. (Forthcoming) find AI-assisted business advice *worsens* outcomes for low-baseline performers in Kenya. The authors flag these and other settings (Roldan 2024 on debate; Kreitmeir & Raschky 2024 on the Italian ChatGPT ban) where AI does *not* level. Benzell & Myers (2026) provide a model rationalizing the heterogeneity through skill-task correlation (p. 5).
- **External validity.** Argentine adults; a single 20-minute task; a generic business scenario; AI access fully optional and free. Real workplaces involve domain-specific knowledge, organizational task assignment, and incentive structures the experiment does not replicate. The authors are careful to position the design as a measurement of a between-group margin, not a forecast of labor-market outcomes (p. 3-4).
- **AI grading concerns.** Although the validation is thorough, LLM grading of LLM-assisted writing has potential biases (e.g., consistency with AI-generated style). Robustness across grading models and human spot-check provides reassurance.
- **Compatibility with autor_2024_rebuild-middle-class.** Autor (2024) (`autor_2024_rebuild-middle-class`) hypothesizes that AI may rebuild the middle class by letting non-elite workers do expert tasks. Cruces et al. provide a clean controlled test of this hypothesis on a general task and largely confirm it for execution — though again with the caveat that underlying human capital remains a separate constraint.
- **Compatibility with Acemoglu's macro view.** Acemoglu (2024) (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`) cautions against extrapolating from large micro-level effects to large macro effects. Cruces et al.'s 1.2 SD low-education gain *is* a large micro-level effect. Acemoglu's caution is consistent with the present paper's own warning that translating task-level leveling into labor-market outcomes requires assumptions about general equilibrium.
