# AI as the Next GPT: A Political-Economy Perspective

**Authors:** Manuel Trajtenberg
**Year:** 2018
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 24245
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/trajtenberg_2018_ai-next-gpt.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/trajtenberg_2018_ai-next-gpt.pdf)
**Source:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w24245

## 1. Full citation

Trajtenberg, Manuel (2018). *AI as the Next GPT: A Political-Economy Perspective*. NBER Working Paper No. 24245, January 2018. JEL: J24, O3, O33, O38.

## 2. Research question

If artificial intelligence is set to become the next General Purpose Technology (GPT), what public policies can mitigate its disruption and amplify its benefits — in a context where the "democratization of expectations" makes the social costs of technological transition politically intolerable? (PDF p. 2, p. 5)

## 3. Method

- **Type of work:** Conceptual essay / political-economy perspective. There is no formal model or original econometric estimation; the author draws on the GPT literature (Bresnahan & Trajtenberg 1995), historical evidence from industrial revolutions (Mokyr 2017), and U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projections (PDF p. 8).
- **Analytical framework:** Trajtenberg combines (i) GPT theory — technologies that generate waves of complementary innovations across an ever-wider range of sectors (PDF p. 2) — with (ii) a political-economy argument about the asymmetry between "winners" and "losers" (PDF pp. 2–4).
- **Key assumptions:** (a) AI will indeed be the next GPT; (b) net job displacement (`x − y`, where `x` is jobs destroyed and `y` is new jobs created) will be *significantly larger* than in previous GPTs because AI can take over even the newly created tasks (PDF p. 4); (c) the demographic transition (aging populations, life expectancy 25–30 years post-retirement) worsens the problem (PDF p. 4).

## 4. Key findings

- **The winners/losers balance has become politically untenable.** Losers tend to be middle-aged, less educated, and concentrated in areas with little employment diversification; post-war safety nets were not designed for permanent structural transformations (PDF p. 3, footnote 5).
- **Asymmetric net displacement.** Unlike previous GPTs, where new occupations offset destroyed ones, in the AI era `y` (new jobs) will be limited because AI can also perform the newly created tasks (PDF p. 4).
- **The "factory model" of education is obsolete.** Trajtenberg documents that average years of schooling in the UK rose from under 1 year in 1870 to more than 13 today, yet the curricular logic remains that of nineteenth-century Prussia (PDF p. 6). Required skills cluster into three types: (I) analytical/creative/adaptive, (II) interpersonal/communicative, (III) emotional/self-confidence (PDF p. 7).
- **Personal services: the bulk of job growth.** According to BLS projections, virtually *all* employment growth in the U.S. through 2024 is concentrated in services; health and social assistance would grow from 18 to 22 million jobs (+20%), while goods production remains flat (~0%) (Table 2, PDF p. 8).
- **Nursing as a precedent.** In 1946 a nurse earned on average 1/3 of the wage of a textile worker (PDF p. 8, note 7); the Nurse Training Act of 1964 professionalized the occupation, and today specialized nurses can earn up to US$100,000 per year (PDF p. 9). This is the model to replicate for early childhood care and other personal services.
- **HEI vs. HRI distinction.** "Human-enhancing innovations" (HEI) — AI-assisted medical diagnosis, da Vinci robotic surgery, personalized teaching — amplify human capabilities; "human-replacing innovations" (HRI) — near-human-free factories like Tesla, or Walmart turning workers into "mindless automatons" — destroy jobs or create undignified work (PDF p. 10). Technology policy should favor HEI over HRI.

## 5. Policy implications (Colombia / Latin America)

- **Early childhood education as a fiscal priority.** Trajtenberg proposes "inverting the pyramid": investing far more in education from ages 0 to 6, citing Heckman (PDF p. 7). In Colombia, this reinforces the logic of programs such as De Cero a Siempre, but demands professionalizing caregivers — currently low-trained and low-paid — through academic credentials in psychology, neurodevelopment, and early detection of learning disabilities (PDF p. 9).
- **Professionalizing care services.** SENA and nursing faculties could lead a "Caregiver Training Law" analogous to the U.S. Nurse Training Act, raising academic standards for nursing auxiliaries, elderly caregivers, and early-childhood educators. This aligns with Colombia's demographic transition (accelerating aging) and the growing demand for personal services that AI will *not* easily replace.
- **Curriculum: from content to skills.** The Ministry of Education's emphasis on socio-emotional competencies should be reinforced with critical thinking, sense-making, and virtual collaboration skills (PDF p. 7). The "factory model" of Colombian secondary schooling — uniform, hierarchical, information-transmitting — is precisely what Trajtenberg considers dysfunctional for the AI era.
- **Industrial policy directed at HEI.** R&D incentives (tax credits, MinCiencias grants) could be directed toward innovations that complement human labor — telemedicine, personalized tutoring, tools for care professionals — rather than toward pure automation.
- **Political economy of the transition.** Trajtenberg warns that without active management of the losers, disruption threatens the democratic fabric (PDF p. 5). In Latin America, where labor informality already exceeds 50% in several countries, this warning is particularly urgent: existing safety nets are even weaker than the European welfare state that Trajtenberg already considers insufficient.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Historical optimism vs. automation pessimism.** Trajtenberg draws on Mokyr (2017) to argue that "gloomy prophecies" rarely materialize, yet simultaneously accepts that net displacement under AI will be greater than in previous GPTs (PDF p. 4). This internal tension is left unresolved. Acemoglu & Restrepo (`acemoglu-restrepo_2018_ai-automation-work`) provide a more formal framework for understanding why AI may not generate enough new tasks.
- **No original quantitative estimates.** Unlike Acemoglu (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`, ~0.66% TFP over 10 years) or Aghion & Bunel (`aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth`, 0.8–1.3 pp/year), Trajtenberg offers no magnitudes; his contribution is normative and framing-oriented, not measurement-oriented.
- **GPT framing possibly premature.** Cockburn, Henderson & Stern (`cockburn-henderson-stern_2018_ai-impact-innovation`), cited by Trajtenberg (PDF p. 2), formally argue that AI is a GPT *of invention* (a method of inventing). Trajtenberg assumes the conclusion without defending it; skeptical readers (Gordon, Summers) might object.
- **Bias toward advanced economies.** All evidence (BLS, UK, Silicon Valley) comes from OECD countries. Direct applicability to economies with segmented and informal labor markets like Colombia requires adaptation — it is not obvious that the "democratization of expectations" operates the same way where the social contract is already fractured.
- **HEI vs. HRI: hard to operationalize.** The distinction is appealing, but the author himself acknowledges that "it is hard to say" whether the direction of technical change can be steered (PDF p. 10). Korinek & Vipra (`korinek-vipra_2025_concentrating-intelligence`) show that market-power concentration in AI can bias the direction toward HRI regardless of policy.
