# A.I. and Our Economic Future

**Authors:** Charles I. Jones (Stanford GSB and NBER)
**Year:** 2026 (Version 1.0, January 15, 2026)
**Venue:** Working paper, prepared for the *Journal of Economic Perspectives*
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/jones_2026_ai-economic-future.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/jones_2026_ai-economic-future.pdf)
**Source:** https://web.stanford.edu/~chadj/AIandEconomicFuture.pdf

## 1. Full citation

Jones, Charles I. (2026). "A.I. and Our Economic Future." Working paper, Stanford GSB and NBER, prepared for the *Journal of Economic Perspectives*.

## 2. Research question

What does economic growth theory have to say about the future of an economy with advanced artificial intelligence, and how should we think simultaneously about its potential macroeconomic gains and its catastrophic risks? Jones organizes the essay around two extreme scenarios — "AI accelerates growth" vs. "AI as business as usual" — and an analytical framework (weak links) that helps distinguish between them (p. 1).

## 3. Method

A theoretical-narrative essay that synthesizes task-based growth models and applies them to AI:

- **Analytical framework:** task-based growth models à la Zeira (1998) and Acemoglu-Restrepo (2018), with the "weak links" variant of Aghion, Jones and Jones (2019) and Jones-Tonetti (2026), where tasks combine with an elasticity of substitution < 1 (an O-ring formulation in the spirit of Kremer) (p. 6).
- **Descriptive data:** real U.S. GDP per capita since 1870 (Figure 1, p. 4), "effective" compute statistics from Epoch AI (p. 2), and AI capability benchmarks (METR, Anthropic) (p. 2).
- **Risk analysis:** expected-utility model with log and CRRA risk aversion, drawing on Jones (2024, 2025), to evaluate trade-offs between growth and existential risk (pp. 12–13).
- **Key assumption:** tasks are complementary (bottleneck structure) — non-automated "weak links" limit the gains from fully automating all other tasks (p. 7).

## 4. Key findings

- **Two plausible scenarios over 25–30 years (one generation).** One features dramatically accelerated growth; the other is business as usual, in which AI is simply the latest general-purpose technology sustaining the historical ~2% annual U.S. rate (Figure 1, p. 4). Both are consistent with available theory and evidence (pp. 2, 5).
- **"Effective" training compute grows ~10× per year** (factor of 4 from chips, factor of 2.5 from algorithms), and the time horizon of tasks AI can solve with 50% success doubles every 5–7 months (p. 2).
- **Surprising weak-links result:** with an elasticity of substitution of 1/2, fully automating a task that accounts for a fraction *s* of GDP raises output only by a factor of 1/(1−s). Because software is ~2% of GDP, automating it infinitely raises GDP by only ~2% (p. 7). Automating all cognitive work (~1/3 of GDP) raises it by ~50% — large, but a one-time level gain, not a permanent rate increase (p. 7).
- **Gradual explosion:** in the Jones-Tonetti (2026) simulation, even though growth rates eventually exceed 5% per year, after 20 years output is only ~5% higher and after 40 years ~20% higher — the weak links "domesticate" the explosion (p. 9).
- **Existential risk and willingness to pay:** with log utility, agents would accept risks of up to 1-in-3 extinction in exchange for 10% growth; with risk aversion of 2 that falls to 2.5%; with medical benefits (mortality halved), agents with risk aversion of 3 would accept a 1-in-4 risk (p. 12).
- **Underinvestment in AI safety:** comparing to the ~4% of GDP the U.S. effectively "spent" to mitigate Covid (a ~0.3% mortality risk), Jones (2025) calculates that we are underinvesting in AI safety by a factor of 30 or more (pp. 13–14).
- **Policy recommendations:** a global tax on GPUs/TPUs at first sale to slow the race and fund safety research (p. 14); international cooperation analogous to the Cold War nuclear regime (p. 15).
- **Calibrated conclusion:** Jones expects AI to have an impact more than 10× that of the internet, but spread over half a century or more (p. 15).

## 5. Policy implications (Colombia / Latin America)

- **Weak links are a warning against automation optimism.** For Colombia, automating cognitive tasks via imported AI does not guarantee productivity leaps if the real bottlenecks lie in logistics infrastructure, energy, governance, or basic education. Complementarity implies that industrial policy should simultaneously address the weakest links — not just the most visible ones — for AI diffusion to translate into GDP gains.
- **Gradual diffusion:** the lesson from David (1990) and Solow (1987) that Jones reiterates (p. 5) implies that for Latin America, where technology adoption typically lags by 10–20 years, expecting immediate productivity gains from AI is unrealistic. Sensible policy invests in organizational complements, connectivity, and intermediate-skills training.
- **Distribution is the central question, not the size of the pie.** Jones argues that an automated world is one of abundance but raises the question: what economically valuable endowment will people have if their labor ceases to be one? (pp. 9–10). He suggests endowing every child with a share in the S&P 500 index (p. 10). For Colombia this resonates with proposals for universal sovereign wealth funds or citizen inheritances via dividends from natural resource revenues or taxes on returns to technological capital.
- **Global compute governance:** the proposed global GPU/TPU tax (p. 14) creates a space where small economies like Colombia can align with international coalitions (EU, chip-export regulation) without needing to compete at the model frontier.
- **Bioactor and surveillance risks:** the catastrophic risks — AI-enabled bioweapons and "alien intelligence" (pp. 11) — are global public goods. Colombia should strengthen its regulatory frameworks and actively participate in multilateral AI governance forums.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Against Acemoglu (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`).** Jones explicitly cites Acemoglu's conservative estimate of a TFP increase of < 0.1pp/year over the next decade (p. 8), presenting it as one end of a spectrum rather than an established truth.
- **Against Aghion-Bunel (`aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth`).** Jones reports that Aghion and Bunel (2024) recalculate that impact at ~0.7pp/year (p. 8), and positions himself closer to Aghion-Jones-Jones (`aghion-jones-jones_2017_ai-economic-growth`), whose formal model can generate explosive growth (p. 5).
- **Jones moderates his own optimism:** despite being a coauthor of the "explosive" 2017/2019 paper, his conclusion here is that weak links dramatically attenuate the explosion (p. 9). This is a theoretically significant self-correction: the result depends crucially on the elasticity of substitution between tasks.
- **Caveat on elasticity:** with Cobb-Douglas (elasticity = 1), a single task with infinite productivity is enough to drive output to infinity (p. 8). The entire gradualist conclusion rests on assuming strong complementarity.
- **Employment section is deliberately brief:** Jones acknowledges that labor market effects are not his area (p. 9) and refers readers to Acemoglu-Restrepo and Autor-Thompson — useful to cross-reference with `acemoglu-restrepo_2018_ai-automation-work` and `autor_2024_rebuild-middle-class`.
- **Existential risk:** Jones's (2024, 2025) calculations on willingness to accept risk are expected-utility exercises with debatable assumptions (CRRA, calibrated medical benefits); they should be read as bounds, not forecasts.
