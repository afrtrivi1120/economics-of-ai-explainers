# AI in Science

**Authors:** Ajay K. Agrawal, John McHale, Alexander Oettl
**Year:** 2026 (March 2026)
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 34953 (chapter prepared for an NBER volume on the science of science)
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/agrawal-mchale-oettl_2026_ai-in-science.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/agrawal-mchale-oettl_2026_ai-in-science.pdf)

## 1. Full citation

Agrawal, A. K., McHale, J., & Oettl, A. (2026). *AI in Science*. NBER Working Paper No. 34953. National Bureau of Economic Research.

## 2. Research question

Two of the most prolific economists writing on growth — Daron Acemoglu and Charles Jones — disagree starkly about how AI will affect economic growth. Acemoglu (2024) (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`) calibrates an upper-bound TFP gain of ~0.55-0.90% over 10 years, explicitly excluding revolutionary effects on the *process* of science within that horizon. Jones (2024) musses about possible AI-enabled growth rates of "perhaps 10 percent per year" (p. 1). The paper asks: how should we think about AI's impact on the *knowledge production function* — the engine of long-run growth — given that AI is a tool that aids search over vast combinatorial spaces of questions, ideas, designs, and tests, while human judgment remains central in many stages? When does AI revolutionize a stage, when does a stage become a bottleneck, and what complementary investments determine whether AI's potential in science is realized? (p. 1-3, 5).

## 3. Method

This is a conceptual / theoretical chapter aimed at synthesizing the economics of AI as it affects scientific productivity. The framework comprises three pieces:

- **Multi-stage scientific productivity model.** Scientific output Ȧ is produced via four stages: (1) question generation (productivity α), (2) idea generation (β), (3) design generation (γ), (4) testing (δ). Total scientific productivity ω = α·β·γ·δ. Following Romer-style endogenous growth, Ȧ = ω·A·S where A is the existing stock of ideas and S the number of scientists (p. 7-9). This decomposition allows AI's effects to differ by stage — a question that the standard one-stage formulation collapses (p. 8).
- **Drug-discovery example.** A scientific team searches over a combinatorial design space X with N_X candidate molecules to find one that effectively binds a target protein. With a generative AI model giving the probability q(x|y=1), expected design-stage productivity is γ — the probability of success on a single AI-guided test. Search ends when a success is found, in expected duration 1/γ (p. 23-24).
- **Task-based augmentation model.** Borrowed from Acemoglu-Restrepo (2018, 2019) (`acemoglu-restrepo_2018_ai-automation-work`), with a twist: the productivity gain from AI scales with the share of the scientific workforce that has *AI expertise* (p. 5-6, Section 5).
- **AI as General Purpose Meta Technology (GPMT).** Building on Cockburn-Henderson-Stern (2018) (`cockburn-henderson-stern_2018_ai-impact-innovation`) and Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb (2019), the authors argue AI is not just a GPT but a *meta*-technology — a technology for developing other technologies. Realizing the gains requires upstream and downstream complementary investments (p. 5, Section 4).
- **Domain case studies.** Material science (Nobel-Prize-winning MOFs work, AI for direct-air capture), physics (theory vs. experiment), computational biology (AlphaFold, ESM3), mathematics (AlphaProof, Lean 4), medicine (drug discovery, abaucin antibiotic), economics (causal-inference ML), computer science / ML research itself (Section 3).
- **Philosophy of science framing.** The authors lean on the distinction between Baconian induction (data-driven question generation) and Peircean surprise (anomaly-driven question generation), drawing on Hacking and Hanson on the role of judgment in seeing with instruments (p. 12-13). This grounds their claim that judgment is a category of effort AI does not eliminate.

## 4. Key findings

- **Both Acemoglu and Jones are partly right — and the model holds them together.** AI is already reshaping the knowledge production function (Jones-aligned), but human judgment remains indispensable at multiple stages, and bottlenecks at any single stage cap aggregate productivity gains (Acemoglu-aligned) (p. 3-4).
- **Stage-by-stage AI roles vary sharply.** Question generation: AI helps via Baconian induction (Ludwig-Mullainathan 2024 mug-shot example) and Peircean surprise (Mullainathan-Rambachan 2024 anomaly-detection in expected utility theory) (p. 11-12). Idea generation: AI explores combinatorial spaces of existing ideas. Design generation: AI's strongest current contribution — AlphaFold for protein structure, GNoME for crystal stability, ESM3 for protein-sequence design (p. 23-24). Testing: AI-aided robotic experimentation, AI-based filtering at the LHC, automated pipelines (p. 25).
- **Domain heterogeneity.** Material science: AI for both Baconian induction (mining patents and simulations) and inverse design (generating MOF candidates for direct air capture from 40 million quantum chemistry simulations) (p. 29-30). Physics: experimentalists use AI to scan datasets for new physical laws (AI Feynman); theorists rely more on judgment. Mathematics: AlphaProof solves competition-level problems; question generation remains human due to its aesthetic dimension. Medicine: dramatic gains in design (AlphaFold) but bottlenecks in clinical-trial testing (δ). Economics: AI for testing causal claims; idea generation bottlenecked by the need for normative significance (p. 30-35).
- **Existence of bottlenecks.** The key model insight: if any one of α, β, γ, δ remains binding, AI improvements in the other stages produce only modest gains in ω. Scientific productivity is the *product*, not the sum, of stage productivities. An AI that revolutionizes design but leaves testing unchanged buys only a stage-specific gain (p. 8, 26).
- **Cavalli (2024) on AlphaFold.** Empirical evidence cited by the authors: the introduction of AlphaFold led specialist labs to *expand* their workforce by hiring more biologists to validate and interpret AI predictions, not shrink it (p. 35). AI complements expert judgment in high-complexity domains.
- **AI as GPMT requires upstream and downstream investment.** Upstream: fundamental AI research (reasoning, agency), scientific compute infrastructure. Downstream: scientist training in AI use, restructured workflows (e.g., autonomous labs), expanded data infrastructure (p. 36-38). Without these complements, the productivity gains stall.
- **Augmentation model implication.** Productivity gains scale with the share of scientists with AI expertise. Investment in the *human* side of the equation — scientist training, curriculum reform, hybrid teams — is therefore a strong determinant of AI's realized scientific impact (p. 38).
- **CEO views vs. economist views.** Amodei (Anthropic), Altman (OpenAI), Hassabis (DeepMind) all forecast a "compressed 21st century" in biomedicine: 50-100 years of progress in 5-10. The authors note these CEOs are closer to the Jones view than the Acemoglu view but discount their views as potentially biased by fundraising and overconfidence (p. 2).

## 5. Policy implications

- **Public R&D investment must be re-directed toward AI complements.** Funding agencies (NIH, NSF, EU framework programs, comparable national funders) should subsidize *complements* to AI: scientific data infrastructure (open data, FAIR data), scientist training in AI use, autonomous-lab equipment, and AI-domain hybrid researchers. Buying more compute or more AI tools without these complements is wasted spend.
- **Bottleneck identification by domain.** A practical screening exercise for funders: for each scientific domain, identify which stage (α, β, γ, δ) currently binds. AI investment at non-binding stages is low-return; at binding stages is high-return. The authors' domain reviews (Section 3) provide a starting bottleneck map.
- **Norm and IP regimes need reform.** As AI plays a larger role in scientific outputs, copyright and patent rules must adapt: how to credit AI-generated discoveries, how to share the rents, how to attribute responsibility for errors. This is a central meta-policy question for science and technology (p. 37).
- **Regulation should preserve augmentation over automation.** The authors are explicit (citing Acemoglu-Johnson 2023 *Power and Progress*) that the normative path of AI development matters for whether it augments or replaces scientists; benchmark design tends to favor automation tasks because they are easier to specify, biasing development (p. 5, footnote 9). Policymakers concerned about science-workforce welfare and concentration of expertise should support augmentation-oriented R&D and benchmark design.
- **AI-expertise gap as policy lever.** Augmentation gains scale with the share of AI-expert scientists. Education and training policy is therefore a *productivity* policy. Curriculum reform — integrating AI tools and methods into PhD training across all scientific disciplines — is a high-return investment (p. 38).
- **Coordination across scientific producers and consumers.** GPMT logic implies that the public benefit of AI in science requires *coordinated* upstream and downstream investments — a coordination failure that markets alone may not solve. International scientific collaborations (e.g., AlphaFold's open release; CERN's open AI-trigger systems) are good models (p. 36-37).
- **Caution on accountability and judgment.** The authors stress that human judgment remains central in tasks involving abductive inference, contextual nuance, and ethical deliberation — exactly the tasks where AI's "jagged frontier" is currently weakest. Policymakers should resist proposals to replace expert evaluation with AI scoring in domains (medicine, criminal justice, public-resource allocation) where judgment is irreducible.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Survey-style chapter, not new estimation.** The paper is conceptual / synthetic. It does not estimate elasticities, does not calibrate the multi-stage model, does not compare alternative parameter values empirically.
- **Disagreement with Acemoglu's bound.** Acemoglu (2024) (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`) is conservative because he restricts attention to AI's effect on the *output* production function over a 10-year horizon. Agrawal et al. argue the *knowledge* production function is the more important channel and that AI's effects there can become transformational over a longer horizon. Jones (2026) (`jones_2026_ai-economic-future`) provides the optimistic case in this volume.
- **Disagreement with Acemoglu-Kong-Ozdaglar 2026.** The companion NBER paper by Acemoglu-Kong-Ozdaglar (`acemoglu-kong-ozdaglar_2026_knowledge-collapse`) emphasizes the *substitution* margin: agentic AI can crowd out human learning effort and trigger long-run knowledge collapse. Agrawal-McHale-Oettl emphasize the *augmentation* margin: AI as a tool used by judgment-exercising scientists. Both papers describe the same parameter space but disagree about the regime in which the world currently sits. Empirical work (such as Cavalli 2024 cited here, or Shen-Tamkin 2026 cited in the companion) is needed to discriminate.
- **Empirical caveat: which stages are binding?** The authors do not provide a quantitative ranking. The bottleneck framing is intuitively powerful but operationally vague: how does a funder *measure* whether testing or idea generation is binding in, say, neuroscience?
- **CEO bias.** Authors explicitly note that frontier-AI CEO predictions ("compressed 21st century") may be optimistically biased due to fundraising incentives (p. 2). This is unusually candid for an NBER chapter.
- **Tension with bloom-et-al firm-data findings.** Bloom, Brynjolfsson and others (`bloom-et-al_2026_firm-data-on-ai`) document modest AI adoption rates so far. Whether scientific productivity gains are large enough to alter long-run growth depends on adoption *and* complementary investment trajectories — which currently appear gradual.
- **Domain-specific bottlenecks may persist for decades.** Clinical trials remain the binding bottleneck in drug discovery regardless of AlphaFold. Regulatory horizons can dominate AI horizons.
