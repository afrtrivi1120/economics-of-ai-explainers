# Financing the AI Buildout

**Author:** Stijn Van Nieuwerburgh (NYU Stern School of Business)
**Year:** 2026 (draft March 19, 2026)
**Venue:** Working Paper
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/van-nieuwerburgh_2026_financing-ai-buildout.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/van-nieuwerburgh_2026_financing-ai-buildout.pdf)
**Source:** https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~svnieuwe/

## 1. Full citation

Van Nieuwerburgh, S. (2026). *Financing the AI Buildout*. Working Paper, NYU Stern School of Business, draft of March 19, 2026.

## 2. Research question

Public discussion of artificial intelligence treats it as an intangible revolution — software, models, and prediction. Van Nieuwerburgh argues this misses the dominant economic feature of the current cycle: AI is, in the near term, a *physical* capital boom. The paper asks three connected questions. First, what is the scale, composition, and physical structure of the AI infrastructure cycle, and how does it compare to historical infrastructure booms (railroads, electrification, telecom)? Second, who is financing it — and how — given that even the largest hyperscalers cannot fund the required investment from operating cash flow alone? Third, what are the resulting risks for the financial system, and what kind of research and policy agenda follows? (p. 1-2, 13-14).

## 3. Method

This is a conceptual / institutional finance paper rather than an estimation exercise. Van Nieuwerburgh assembles industry data from Brandsaas et al. (Federal Reserve, 2025) on project-level data center investment, S&P Global / Moody's / Morgan Stanley research on capital structure of AI infrastructure deals, Green Street's data-center sector outlook, and case-study transactional documents (notably the Hyperion / Beignet financing involving Meta and Blue Owl). The paper also leans on the public-finance and securitization literatures (DeMarzo 2005; Coval, Jurek and Stafford 2009) and on the data-economy literature (Farboodi & Veldkamp 2026; Jones & Tonetti 2020) to interpret the patterns. It is descriptive and analytical, not causal — but it is grounded in specific transactions and disclosed financial structures.

## 4. Key findings

- **Scale.** A contemporary 200-MW hyperscale AI campus implies roughly **$8.2 billion** of capital cost, of which one-third is real estate plus power infrastructure ($2.2B + $0.4B) and two-thirds is IT equipment ($5.6B), assuming GPU servers cost ~$40 million per MW (p. 3). This is a sharp departure from earlier data-center economics, in which the building dominated the bill.
- **Aggregate buildout.** U.S. data-center capacity could grow by as much as **200 GW between 2026 and 2032**, on top of an installed base of ~50 GW and 36 GW under construction (p. 4). Multiplying through gives roughly **$8.2 trillion** in cumulative AI infrastructure investment, around **2.8% of GDP per year** for eight years (p. 4). For context: U.S. railroad investment 1865-1890 averaged 2.4% of GDP, electrification 1905-1925 was 1.1%, the interstate highway system 1956-1973 was 1.6%, telecom/fiber 1996-2003 was 0.8% (p. 4). The AI cycle is bigger than each of these.
- **Capex absorbs ever more cash flow.** Among the five largest hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Google, Oracle), capex jumped from ~$262B in 2020 to ~$728B (estimated) in 2026, with the capex/operating-cash-flow ratio rising from ~40% to ~85% (Figure 1, p. 6).
- **External financing dominates.** Morgan Stanley estimates that **more than half** of the ~$2.9 trillion in additional compute capacity required over 2025-2028 will be funded externally (p. 7). Aggregate split is roughly **60% equity / 40% debt**; within the debt, **~70% is private credit (≈$800B)**, ~17% corporate debt, ~13% structured finance (p. 7).
- **High asset-level leverage.** Although hyperscaler balance-sheet leverage looks modest (Meta: 25% book leverage, 4% market leverage at end-2025; p. 8 footnote 3), once you look through to the project-finance vehicles that hold the data centers, leverage ratios run **70-80%** and can hit **90% or more** (p. 7-8). The Hyperion / Beignet deal — Meta sold an 80% stake to Blue Owl for $2.5B, the joint venture then raised $27B in external debt against $30B of asset value, A+ rated (one notch below Meta) — is the showcase example (p. 8).
- **Off-balance-sheet exposures are large and growing.** Moody's finds hyperscalers carry roughly **$970 billion in lease commitments**, of which **$660 billion is not on balance sheet** under current GAAP rules (p. 10). Beignet's debt was issued at 6.58% — at least 100 basis points above what Meta would pay on equivalent unsecured debt — translating to >$5 billion in additional interest expense over the bond's life. Meta accepts this premium because off-balance-sheet financing preserves the equity-valuation multiples of a growth-software firm (p. 10).
- **Asset-class beta has risen.** The estimated stock-market beta of publicly traded data-center REITs has moved from roughly **0.5 historically to about 1 today**, indicating these assets now co-move with the broader equity market — they are no longer the defensive infrastructure investments earlier investors were accustomed to (p. 12).
- **Five risk channels.** (i) Concentration / counterparty: many data centers are single-tenant facilities for one of a small number of hyperscalers, themselves dependent on a small number of model developers and enterprise clients. (ii) Technological obsolescence: chips, cooling, and architecture evolve fast — long-duration assets may have shorter effective economic lives than financing assumes (p. 11). (iii) Power and permitting: grid interconnection, electricity prices, and local regulatory opposition can stall projects (p. 11). (iv) Supply chain: chip fabrication is geographically concentrated (TSMC) and geopolitically exposed (p. 11-12). (v) Financial structure: high leverage, originate-to-distribute lending in data-center mortgages (Gete & Mercader 2026), and complex layered claims can amplify losses if expectations shift (p. 12).

## 5. Policy implications

- **Measurement comes first.** The single most important conclusion of the paper is that the *AI infrastructure exposure* of large technology firms cannot be read off their balance sheets. Lease commitments, residual-value guarantees, joint-venture stakes, and off-balance-sheet SPVs collectively obscure the true capital intensity of AI (p. 13-14). Securities regulators (SEC, FASB, and international counterparts), prudential regulators, and macro-financial monitors should fund work to map the entire capital stack — from compute users through hyperscalers, data-center owners, private credit funds, and securitization vehicles — to identify where ultimate exposure resides.
- **Accounting standards.** GAAP rules were not designed for long-duration infrastructure financed through short-term leases, contingent guarantees, and off-balance-sheet entities. Closing the disclosure gap (e.g., requiring on-balance-sheet recognition of residual-value guarantees and contingent lease commitments) would materially improve credit-risk pricing and investor decision-making.
- **Macroprudential surveillance.** Rapid, capital-intensive expansions financed with growing leverage and complex structured-finance claims have historically been followed by financial stress when demand or financing conditions shift (p. 13). The AI ecosystem has interlocking circular relationships — chip designers, cloud providers, model developers, data-center operators — so a shock in one segment can propagate. National macroprudential authorities should add AI infrastructure exposure to their surveillance dashboards.
- **Energy and permitting policy.** Because power, transmission, and grid interconnection are now binding constraints on AI compute expansion (p. 5), energy and electric-utility regulators are *de facto* AI regulators. Coordination between energy and competition authorities (already visible in the form of state-level data-center subsidies documented by Gargano & Giacoletti 2025; p. 5) will determine whether the buildout is realized at projected scale.
- **Wealth distribution.** Kogan, Papanikolaou and Stoffman (2020) showed that financial markets only partially hedge households against technological displacement because innovators must retain equity stakes for incentive reasons. Van Nieuwerburgh argues the AI infrastructure boom raises the same issue: the most valuable claims (hyperscaler equity, large private-credit funds) are concentrated, while diffuse retail investors hold mostly low-yield infrastructure debt (p. 14). Broad participation through public markets, pension funds, and infrastructure ETFs is one channel through which households can hedge labor-displacement risk; whether that channel is large enough is an open question.
- **Universal-service / antitrust hooks.** When private credit, REITs, and securitization vehicles own the physical layer that runs frontier AI models, the ownership of compute becomes a quasi-regulated infrastructure question rather than purely a corporate-finance question — analogous to railroads, electricity, and telecom in their boom phases.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Boom vs. bust framing.** Van Nieuwerburgh is explicit that the paper is *not* predicting an imminent financial crisis (p. 12-13). Several optimistic scenarios — sustained demand for AI compute, continued model-capability scaling, governments investing in AI infrastructure as strategic — could keep the buildout productive. The paper's claim is that risk is being redistributed and amplified, not that it has already materialized.
- **Tension with macro-AI growth literature.** Aghion-Bunel (2024) (`aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth`) and Jones (2026) (`jones_2026_ai-economic-future`) argue AI could substantially raise long-run growth. If their optimistic scenarios are correct, the AI capital stock will be *underbuilt* relative to demand and the financing risks Van Nieuwerburgh identifies will not bite. Acemoglu (2024) (`acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`) gives the conservative bound; under that view, the buildout is more likely to overshoot. The financing-risk question is therefore directly tied to the macro-impact debate.
- **Comparison with korinek-vipra concentration story.** Korinek & Vipra (2025) (`korinek-vipra_2025_concentrating-intelligence`) emphasize that scaling laws concentrate AI model development in a few firms. Van Nieuwerburgh emphasizes that the *financial* layer is also concentrated (a small number of hyperscalers as tenants, a few private-credit funds as lenders). The two papers describe complementary forms of concentration that compound counterparty risk.
- **Historical analogies are imperfect.** Railroads and telecom went through bust cycles that left useful infrastructure for later use. Whether GPU-heavy data centers retain residual value through technological transitions (e.g., to inference-edge hardware or quantum) is uncertain; obsolescence risk on AI-specialized assets is structurally different from track gauge or fiber strands.
- **Limited theory.** The paper is descriptive and institutional; it does not estimate financial-stability elasticities or run shock simulations. The author flags this as part of the research agenda (p. 13-14).
