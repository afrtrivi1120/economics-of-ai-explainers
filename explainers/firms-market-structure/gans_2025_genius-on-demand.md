**Authors:** Ajay K. Agrawal, Joshua S. Gans, Avi Goldfarb
**Year:** 2025
**Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 34316
**PDF:** [papers/pdfs/gans_2025_genius-on-demand.pdf](../../papers/pdfs/gans_2025_genius-on-demand.pdf)

## 1. Full citation

Agrawal, Ajay K., Joshua S. Gans, and Avi Goldfarb (2025). *Genius on Demand: The Value of Transformative Artificial Intelligence*. NBER Working Paper No. 34316, October. JEL: D24, J24, O33.

## 2. Research question

How would the labor market absorb an "avalanche" of genius-level cognitive capacity provided by transformative AI — what Amodei (2024) calls "a country of geniuses in a data center" — and what would the consequences be for different types of knowledge workers, in both the short and long run? (PDF p. 3).

## 3. Method

- **Theoretical model**, not empirical. Builds on Carnehl & Schneider (2025), which represents knowledge as question-answer pairs distributed on the real line, with the true answer generated by a standard Brownian motion (PDF p. 5).
- **Three types of agents**: (i) routine workers, who apply existing knowledge with variance rising in distance |x − x₀| from the known point; (ii) human geniuses, who create new knowledge at quadratic cost Cost(x) = η·(x − x₀)² (PDF p. 9); (iii) a manager who routes each question to the appropriate worker type (PDF p. 11).
- **Value function**: utility follows a quadratic loss 1 − (a(x) − y(x))²/q, with a precision parameter q that defines the threshold above which routine workers abstain (PDF p. 6).
- **Key assumptions**: uniform domain [0,1] with x₀ = ½ and ½ > q; routine workers are abundant (L_R ≥ 2q) but geniuses are scarce (L_G < 1); η ≤ 4 so that geniuses can cover the full domain (PDF pp. 6–9).
- **AI**: introduced as an autonomous "genius" with a symmetric cost function but potentially lower efficiency, η_AI ≥ η, and effectively unlimited supply (PDF pp. 14–15).
- **Comparative statics**: short run (routine assignments fixed due to rigidities) vs. long run (full re-optimization).

## 4. Key findings

- **Optimal assignment without AI**: when geniuses are scarce, they must first be assigned to questions with the highest *absolute advantage* AA(x) = V_G(x) − V_R(x). This does NOT occur at the most distant points (where their cost is highest) but at the edges of the routine domain, where routine workers can no longer answer but the genius's cost is still moderate (PDF p. 11).
- **Two regimes by η**: if η ≤ 1/(2q²), the scarce genius capacity goes first to the edges x₀ ± q ("edge-first"); if η > 1/(2q²), it goes to interior bands at x₀ ± 1/(2ηq) ("interior-first"). In both cases the bands widen as L_G increases (PDF pp. 12–13).
- **Short run with AI (Proposition 2)**: a three-tier assignment emerges — human geniuses specialize in questions farthest from existing knowledge (where their advantage over AI, (η_AI − η)·(x − x₀)², is greatest); AI covers intermediate questions and fills in previously unanswered zones; routine workers retain their pre-AI assignments (PDF pp. 15–16).
- **Long run (Proposition 3)**: if η_AI is sufficiently close to η, **routine workers are fully displaced** from knowledge work. The exact condition is η_AI ≤ 1/q²; when met, AI replaces routine labor across the entire Region 2 (PDF p. 18).
- **Extreme specialization of human talent**: in the long run, human geniuses are assigned exclusively to questions with |x − x₀| ≥ d*, where d* is pinned by the capacity constraint L_G (PDF p. 18). Human talent thus becomes "ultra-specialized" at the most novel frontier.
- **Expansion of the answerable domain**: questions in Regions 1 and 3 that previously went unanswered now receive answers from AI or human geniuses, widening the total set of problems the economy can address (PDF pp. 18–19).

## 5. Policy implications (Colombia / Latin America)

- **The "floor" of knowledge work is the most exposed, not the ceiling.** The model predicts that the most vulnerable group is not the creative expert but the routine worker who applies codified knowledge: paralegals, medical residents doing standard diagnosis, developers implementing known algorithms (PDF p. 7). In Colombia, this covers a large share of formal urban white-collar employment — legal services, accounting, BPO — sectors that have driven professional job growth over the past 15 years.
- **Education policy: invest in the "frontier," not just "application."** If the surviving human geniuses concentrate on questions with large |x − x₀|, the returns to doctoral programs, applied research, and frontier-specialist training (specialized medicine, innovation law, research engineering) rise relatively. Mass expansion of undergraduate programs geared toward routine office tasks may face diminishing returns.
- **Computing capacity and access to "genius" AI.** The model assumes unlimited AI-genius supply once developed (PDF p. 15). For Latin America, with no technological frontier of its own, the policy question is access (price, data sovereignty, contracts with foreign providers) rather than development. Without cheap access, the relevant regime is a high η_AI and routine displacement is slow or partial.
- **Labor transition**: the short/long-run distinction (PDF pp. 15, 17) suggests the adjustment is not instantaneous — organizational rigidities temporarily protect routine workers. That window is the opportunity for active (not passive) retraining of the professional workforce.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **Pure substitution model vs. complementarity models.** The paper itself acknowledges in the conclusion that it models AI as an "independent agent that replaces" and that this "may not capture the most promising potential" (PDF p. 18). The same authors, in `agrawal-gans-goldfarb_2025_bicycles-for-the-mind`, explore the opposite view — AI as a complementary tool, not an autonomous substitute. Both perspectives coexist within the same research team.
- **Tractability vs. realism.** The model assumes a single knowledge point, uniform question distribution, quadratic cost, and an omniscient manager over worker capabilities (PDF pp. 5–11). There is no within-type heterogeneity among "routine" or "genius" workers, and no Garicano-style hierarchies.
- **No empirical numbers.** Unlike `acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai` (which estimates ~0.66% TFP gain over 10 years) or `aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth` (0.8–1.3 percentage points per year), there is no macro calibration here. Predictions are qualitative: the direction of assignment, not the magnitude of the impact on employment or GDP.
- **Assumes η_AI ≥ η.** If AI were more efficient than humans even at the frontier (η_AI < η), the model predicts immediate and indiscriminate substitution of all human geniuses (PDF p. 14, note 5). The paper rules this case out by assumption, not by evidence.
- **Comparison with Ide & Talamas (2025)**: both address AI in the knowledge economy, but Ide-Talamas uses differences in workers' *knowledge stocks* (Garicano-style hierarchy), while this paper uses differences in the *mode of processing* existing knowledge (PDF p. 4). The distributional predictions may differ.
