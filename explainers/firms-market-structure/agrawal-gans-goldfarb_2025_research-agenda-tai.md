## 1. Full citation

Brynjolfsson, E., Korinek, A., & Agrawal, A. K. (2025). *A Research Agenda for the Economics of Transformative AI*. NBER Working Paper No. 34256. National Bureau of Economic Research. https://www.nber.org/papers/w34256

## 2. Research question

What are the urgent economic questions the profession must answer before *Transformative AI* (TAI) arrives — defined as AI capable of sustaining total factor productivity (TFP) growth 3 to 5 times the historical average? The authors propose a structured agenda of nine "Grand Challenges" to guide research and policy design (PDF p. 2).

## 3. Method

- **Document type:** programmatic essay / research agenda, not an empirical study. It does not estimate new parameters; it synthesizes the literature and structures open questions.
- **Analytical framework:** an operational definition of TAI tied to a TFP growth threshold of 3–5x historical averages (PDF p. 5), and an illustrative benchmark — *"a country of geniuses in a datacenter"* (Amodei) — as a capabilities reference (PDF p. 5).
- **Three-level analytical structure:** (1) how TAI affects innovation and idea creation, (2) how it reorganizes factors of production, business models, and institutions, and (3) how it substitutes or complements human labor and cognitive capital (PDF p. 4).
- **Output:** nine Grand Challenges with 21 numbered research questions (PDF p. 6) and a four-pillar methodological proposal: theory, a Transformative AI Dashboard, new welfare metrics, and empirical work supported by AI itself (PDF pp. 14–15).
- **Key assumption:** even if the probability of TAI were low, the consequences justify scenario planning (PDF p. 4, note 2).

## 4. Key findings

- **Quantitative definition of TAI:** an AI system that sustains TFP growth of at least 3–5 times historical averages (PDF p. 5). An alternative metric cited from Davidson (2023) places the threshold at ~30% annual output growth, roughly 10x the industrial-era average (PDF p. 5).
- **Compressed timelines:** the Metaculus median for the arrival of a "general AI system" shifted from 2062 (in 2020) to 2033 at the time of writing, with a quarter of participants expecting it by 2028 (PDF p. 3). Amodei suggests ~2026; Hassabis "within the decade" (PDF p. 3).
- **Potential labor impact:** citing Eloundou et al. (2024), current generative AI systems would significantly affect at least half the tasks of 19% of U.S. workers, and 80% of jobs would see ≥10% of their tasks affected (PDF p. 3).
- **Nine Grand Challenges:** economic growth; invention and innovation; income distribution; concentration of decision-making power; geoeconomics; information and knowledge; safety and alignment; meaning and wellbeing; transition dynamics (PDF p. 6).
- **Challenge for emerging economies:** the agenda explicitly raises the question of what policies countries with limited technological capacity can adopt to avoid marginalization in the global TAI economy (PDF p. 10).
- **Acknowledged disagreement:** the agenda explicitly notes that Acemoglu (2025) and Narayanan & Kapoor (2025) hold that AI will have modest impacts, but argues that scenario planning is warranted regardless (PDF p. 4, note 2).

## 5. Policy implications

- **Diagnostic framework for policy design:** the agenda gives policymakers a ready-made taxonomy for structuring national AI plans — the nine challenges serve as a checklist to verify whether a national strategy covers productivity, distribution, governance, safety, and transition.
- **Explicit geoeconomic risk:** Section 3.5 directly raises the problem of technological marginalization for lower-capacity countries (PDF p. 10). This reinforces the relevance of investments in sovereign compute, curated public data, doctoral training in AI, and technology-sharing initiatives through multilateral bodies — especially for non-frontier economies.
- **Social safety nets:** the authors emphasize that social insurance schemes designed on the assumption that labor is the primary source of income may cease to function (PDF p. 9). In many developing economies — where informal employment is prevalent and contributory social protection systems cover only part of the workforce — this suggests accelerating debate on non-contributory protection floors before AI-induced employment shocks materialize.
- **Transition dashboard:** the proposed "Transformative AI Dashboard" (PDF p. 15) can be translated into a regional or multilateral observatory that monitors AI adoption, substitution, and productivity indicators across emerging markets.
- **Education and human capital policy:** the paper asks which human skills become redundant and which remain valuable, and how education systems should adapt (PDF p. 7) — a question directly actionable for ministries of education and vocational training systems worldwide.

## 6. Debates and caveats

- **TAI vs. "modest macro":** the agenda assumes TFP growth thresholds of 3–5x historical averages (PDF p. 5), a stance aligned with `aghion-bunel_2024_ai-and-growth` (0.8–1.3 pp/yr) and `aghion-jones-jones_2017_ai-economic-growth` (singularity under substitution), but far from `acemoglu_2024_simple-macro-ai`, whose ceiling of ~0.66% cumulative TFP over 10 years would make the "transformative" scenario unrecognizable. The authors explicitly cite this disagreement (PDF p. 4, note 2).
- **Direct connection to Jones (2024) on safety vs. growth:** Section 3.7 frames the growth-vs.-catastrophic-risk trade-off drawing on Jones (PDF p. 12) — see `jones_2026_ai-economic-future` for the formal treatment of that trade-off.
- **Concentration:** the agenda supports the empirical question of whether TAI will be dominated by one, few, or many systems (PDF p. 9), explicitly citing Korinek & Vipra — see `korinek-vipra_2025_concentrating-intelligence`.
- **AI as a research tool:** the methodological section converges with `korinek_2025_ai-agents-econ-research` in positing that TAI itself will transform empirical practice in economics.
- **Formal caveat:** this document does *not* contribute new evidence. It is a framework of questions; its quantitative figures are almost entirely drawn from third-party citations (Eloundou, Metaculus, Davidson). Any use must respect that attribution chain.
- **Absence of the Global South:** although the geoeconomics section mentions countries with low technological capacity (PDF p. 10), the agenda does not elaborate on concrete catch-up mechanisms — a gap that research from emerging economies can fill.
