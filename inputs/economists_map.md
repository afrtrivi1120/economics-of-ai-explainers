# A Map of Leading Academic Economists Working on the Economics of AI (United States & Europe)

*Prepared for a Senior Researcher at CEPE (Colombia), April 2026*

This reference map profiles the top academic economists in the United States and Europe who are actively shaping the economics of artificial intelligence (AI). It covers five sub-areas: (1) AI and labor markets/productivity; (2) AI and firm behavior/market structure; (3) AI in education; (4) AI and inequality/welfare; and (5) economics of AI models themselves (training costs, scaling, governance, industry structure). For each scholar I provide current primary affiliation, sub-area(s), 2–4 representative AI-related works, methodological approach, and notable policy/public engagement.

Each entry is tagged with the relevant sub-areas: **[L]** = labor/productivity, **[F]** = firms/market structure, **[E]** = education, **[I]** = inequality/welfare, **[M]** = economics of AI models/industry/governance. Inclusion prioritizes scholars who are highly-cited, leading the agenda, and affiliated with top-tier institutions, NBER and/or CEPR.

## I. Senior Figures Defining the Field

### 1. Daron Acemoglu — MIT (Institute Professor, Department of Economics) **[L, I, M]**
2024 Nobel Laureate; arguably the most influential economist on AI and labor. Develops task-based macro models of automation and AI.
- "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI," *Economic Policy* (2025) / NBER WP 32487 (2024) — argues AI will raise US TFP by at most ~0.66% over 10 years [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487).
- With Simon Johnson, *Power and Progress: Our Thousand-Year Struggle Over Technology and Prosperity* (PublicAffairs, 2023) [Source](https://shapingwork.mit.edu/power-and-progress/).
- "Artificial Intelligence, Automation and Work" with P. Restrepo, NBER WP 24196 (2018) [Source](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3098384).
- "AI and Social Media: A Political Economy Perspective," with Ozdaglar & Siderius, NBER (2025) [Source](https://ideas.repec.org/e/pac16.html).
- "AI, Human Cognition and Knowledge Collapse," with Kong & Ozdaglar, NBER WP 34910 (2026) — dynamic model in which agentic AI substitutes for human learning effort and can drive a long-run knowledge-collapse steady state; derives a two-phase optimal information-design regulation [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34910).
- **Method:** Task-based theoretical macro models, calibration, empirical work on robots.
- **Public engagement:** Extensive op-eds (Project Syndicate, NYT), IMF F&D articles, warnings about excessive automation and need for "pro-worker AI" [Source](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/fandd/issues/2023/12/rebalancing-ai-acemoglu-johnson).

### 2. David Autor — MIT (Ford Professor of Economics) **[L, I]**
Foremost labor economist of technological change; co-director of MIT Shaping the Future of Work.
- "Applying AI to Rebuild Middle Class Jobs," NBER WP 32140 (2024) — argues AI could re-expand the middle class by letting non-elite workers do expert tasks [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140).
- "New Frontiers: The Origins and Content of New Work, 1940–2018," with Chin, Salomons & Seegmiller, *QJE* (2024) [Source](https://microeconomicinsights.org/the-race-between-automation-and-new-work/).
- "The Labor Market Impacts of Technological Change," Brookings/NBER WP 30074 (2022).
- "Is Automation Labor-Displacing?" with Salomons, *BPEA* (2018) [Source](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/is-automation-labor-displacing-productivity-growth-employment-and-the-labor-share/).
- **Method:** Task framework; empirical labor economics with administrative and patent data.
- **Public engagement:** National Academies panel on AI and the Future of Work (2024); *Possible* and *Social Science Space* podcasts; *NOEMA*/HBR essays [Source](https://issues.org/david-autor-economist-ai-future-work/).

### 3. Erik Brynjolfsson — Stanford (Jerry Yang & Akiko Yamazaki Professor; Director, Stanford Digital Economy Lab) **[L, F, M, I]**
The economist who first brought "Economics of AI" into the mainstream; founded the Stanford Digital Economy Lab and co-chairs the Economics of Transformative AI initiative.
- "Generative AI at Work," with D. Li & L. Raymond, *QJE* (2025), NBER WP 31161 — first large-scale field study of GenAI in a workplace, showing 14% average productivity gain and 34% for novices [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161).
- *The Economics of Transformative AI* (U. Chicago Press, 2025), with Agrawal & Korinek [Source](https://www.brynjolfsson.com/).
- "The Turing Trap: The Promise and Peril of Human-Like AI," *Daedalus* (2022).
- "Firm Data on AI," NBER WP 34836 (with Yotzov, Bloom, Bunn, Davis et al., 2026) — cross-country survey of ~6000 firms [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34836).
- **Method:** Field experiments, large-scale surveys, "productivity J-curve" framework, macro measurement (GDP-B).
- **Public engagement:** Co-chaired National Academies' "AI and the Future of Work" (2024); co-founder AI Index; testified before US Congress; WorkLab and HBS podcasts [Source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik_Brynjolfsson).

### 4. Pascual Restrepo — Yale (Associate Professor of Economics) **[L, I]**
Acemoglu's key coauthor on automation; now at Yale after Boston University.
- With D. Acemoglu, "The Race Between Man and Machine," *AER* (2018); "Automation and New Tasks," *JEP* (2019) [Source](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.33.2.3).
- "Automation: Theory, Evidence, and Outlook," *Annual Review of Economics* (2024).
- "We Won't Be Missed: Work and Growth in the AGI World" (2024) — theory of economies where AI can perform nearly any task [Source](https://economics.yale.edu/news/260317/pascual-restrepo-ai-automation-and-future-work).
- "Automation and Rent Dissipation," NBER WP 32536 (2024).
- **Method:** Theoretical task-based models combined with empirical robot/AI exposure studies.

### 5. Philippe Aghion — Collège de France, LSE, INSEAD **[L, F, M]**
Endogenous growth theorist; in 2024 led the Nobel shortlist conversation on growth and AI.
- "Artificial Intelligence and Economic Growth," with B. Jones & C. Jones, in *The Economics of Artificial Intelligence: An Agenda* (U. Chicago, 2019) / NBER WP 23928 [Source](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3053718).
- "How Different Uses of AI Shape Labor Demand: Evidence from France," with Bunel, Jaravel, Mikaelsen, Roulet, Søgaard, *AEA P&P* (2025) [Source](https://eprints.lse.ac.uk/128375/).
- "AI and Growth: Where Do We Stand?" with Bunel (SF Fed, 2024) [Source](https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/AI-and-Growth-Aghion-Bunel.pdf).
- **Method:** Schumpeterian growth theory; increasingly combined with firm-level French microdata.
- **Public engagement:** Advisor to French presidents and the European Commission; co-editor of *The Economics of Creative Destruction* (Harvard UP, 2022).

### 6. Susan Athey — Stanford GSB (Economics of Technology Professor) **[F, M]**
Pioneered machine-learning methods for causal inference; 2025 AEA presidential address focused on AI as designer tool.
- "Machine Learning Methods That Economists Should Know About," with G. Imbens, *Annual Review of Economics* (2019) [Source](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/faculty/susan-athey).
- "Recursive Partitioning for Heterogeneous Causal Effects" (PNAS, 2016); "Estimation and Inference of Heterogeneous Treatment Effects Using Random Forests" (JASA, 2018).
- AEA Presidential Address: "The Economist as Designer in the Innovation Process for Socially Impactful Digital Products," *AER* (2025) [Source](https://gsb-faculty.stanford.edu/susan-athey/research/).
- "The Economics of Algorithmic Personalization: Evidence from an Educational Technology Platform," with K. Agrawal, Kanodia, Nath & Palikot, NBER WP 34950 (2026) — RCT-then-RDD evaluation of a collaborative-filtering recommender on an Indian children's reading app; documents a *reverse* voltage drop where scaled effects exceed RCT estimates by 2.5× [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34950).
- **Method:** Machine learning + causal inference; generalized random forests; large-scale field experiments.
- **Public engagement:** Former Chief Economist at Microsoft; works with nonprofits on AI deployment; extremely influential in the econ + ML community. Highly relevant for the user's interest in causal inference and AI [Source](https://mdi.georgetown.edu/news/mdi-fall-2025-distinguished-lecture-ai-causal-inference-impact/).

### 7. Joseph Stiglitz — Columbia University **[I, M]**
Nobel laureate; prominent voice on AI regulation and distribution.
- "Artificial Intelligence and Its Implications for Income Distribution and Unemployment," with A. Korinek, in *The Economics of Artificial Intelligence: An Agenda* (U. Chicago, 2019).
- "Steering Technological Progress," with Korinek, INET (2025).
- *The Road to Freedom: Economics and the Good Society* (2024) — sustained arguments for regulating AI to protect against inequality and disinformation [Source](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/unregulated-ai-will-worsen-inequality-warns-nobel-winning-economist-joseph-stiglitz/).
- **Method:** Welfare economics / political economy.

### 8. Simon Johnson — MIT Sloan (Ronald A. Kurtz Professor of Entrepreneurship) **[L, I, M]**
2024 Nobel laureate with Acemoglu; co-director of MIT Stone Center on Inequality and Shaping the Future of Work.
- *Power and Progress*, with Acemoglu (PublicAffairs, 2023) [Source](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/faculty/directory/simon-johnson).
- "Learning from Ricardo and Thompson: Machinery and Labor in the Early Industrial Revolution, and in the Age of AI," NBER WP 32416 (2024).
- "Can We Have Pro-Worker AI?" with Acemoglu & Autor (2024) [Source](https://shapingwork.mit.edu/about-us/simon-johnson/).
- **Public engagement:** Former IMF Chief Economist; UK government "AI Ambassador" (2025); presentations to SF Fed EERN [Source](https://www.frbsf.org/news-and-media/events/2024/07/simon-johnson-technology-and-inequality-in-the-age-of-ai/).

## II. The "Prediction Machines" Trio (University of Toronto, Rotman)

### 9. Ajay Agrawal — Rotman School of Management, U. Toronto **[F, M, L]**
Founder of the Creative Destruction Lab and co-organizer of the NBER Economics of AI conferences.
- *Prediction Machines: The Simple Economics of Artificial Intelligence* (HBR Press, 2018; updated 2022) and *Power and Prediction* (2022), both with Gans and Goldfarb [Source](https://www.amazon.com/Prediction-Machines-Economics-Artificial-Intelligence/dp/1633695670).
- *The Economics of Artificial Intelligence: An Agenda* (U. Chicago Press, 2019), co-edited with Gans and Goldfarb — the foundational volume in the field [Source](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo35780726.html).
- *The Economics of Transformative AI* (U. Chicago Press, 2025), co-edited with Brynjolfsson and Korinek.
- "A Research Agenda for the Economics of Transformative AI," NBER WP 34256 (2025).
- "AI in Science," with John McHale & Alexander Oettl, NBER WP 34953 (2026) — multi-stage model of scientific productivity treating AI as a General Purpose Meta Technology; argues human judgment remains central in question generation, idea generation, and abductive inference [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34953).
- **Method:** Applied micro / IO; framing AI as "cheap prediction."

### 10. Joshua Gans — Rotman, U. Toronto **[F, M]**
Same trio; Chief Economist of Creative Destruction Lab.
- *Prediction Machines* and *Power and Prediction* (with Agrawal & Goldfarb).
- "The Economics of Bicycles for the Mind," with Agrawal & Goldfarb, NBER WP 34034 (2025) [Source](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5347891).
- "Genius on Demand: The Value of Transformative AI," NBER WP 34316 (2025).
- **Method:** Applied theory (IO, innovation, judgment-vs-prediction framework).
- **Public engagement:** *Digitopoly* blog, *FT*, *HBR*, *Slate* op-eds.

### 11. Avi Goldfarb — Rotman, U. Toronto (Rotman Chair in AI & Healthcare) **[F, M, L]**
Same trio; prolific empirical work on digital economy and AI adoption patterns.
- *Prediction Machines* and *Power and Prediction*.
- "Artificial Intelligence Adoption and System-Wide Change," *JEMS* (2024) [Source](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jems.12521).
- "Could Machine Learning Be a General-Purpose Technology? Evidence from Online Job Postings," with Taska & Teodoridis.
- "Pause Artificial Intelligence Research?" *CJE* (2024).
- **Method:** Applied empirical IO; often uses job postings / labor data [Source](https://www.avigoldfarb.com/artificial-intelligence).

## III. Economics of AI Models, Transformative AI, and Governance

### 12. Anton Korinek — University of Virginia (Department of Economics & Darden School) **[M, I, L]**
Faculty Director of the Economics of Transformative AI (EconTAI) Initiative; arguably the leading economist thinking systematically about AGI/TAI.
- Editor, *Oxford Handbook of AI Governance* (2024).
- "Economic Growth under Transformative AI," with P. Trammell, *Annual Review of Economics* (forthcoming 2026) [Source](https://philiptrammell.com/).
- "Concentrating Intelligence: Scaling and Market Structure in Artificial Intelligence," with J. Vipra, *Economic Policy* (2025), NBER WP 33139 [Source](https://ideas.repec.org/e/pko142.html).
- "Steering Technological Progress," with Stiglitz (INET, 2025); "AI Agents for Economic Research," NBER WP 34202 (2025).
- **Method:** Macro theory; scaling-laws-informed modeling; welfare analysis.
- **Public engagement:** *TIME100 AI* (2025); Anthropic Economic Advisory Council; testified before US Senate; *Coursera* "Economics of AI"; advises G7, Brookings, Peterson Institute [Source](https://www.darden.virginia.edu/faculty-research/directory/anton-korinek).

### 13. Philip Trammell — Stanford Digital Economy Lab (postdoc with Brynjolfsson and Jones); University of Oxford **[M, I]**
Rising star on theoretical implications of transformative AI.
- "Economic Growth under Transformative AI," with Korinek, GPI/NBER WP (2023, forthcoming AROE 2026) [Source](https://philiptrammell.com/).
- "Existential Risk and Growth," with L. Aschenbrenner; "Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI," with Karger, Tetlock et al.
- **Method:** Growth theory, decision theory.

### 14. Charles I. Jones — Stanford GSB **[M, L]**
Leading growth economist engaging with AI.
- "A.I. and Our Economic Future" (2026 working paper) [Source](https://web.stanford.edu/~chadj/AIandEconomicFuture.pdf).
- "Artificial Intelligence and Economic Growth," with Aghion & B. Jones (NBER WP 23928, 2017).
- "The AI Dilemma: Growth vs. Existential Risk," NBER WP (2023).
- **Method:** Endogenous growth theory; idea production functions.

### 15. Ufuk Akcigit — University of Chicago (Arnold C. Harberger Professor of Economics) **[F, M]**
Macroeconomist of innovation and firm dynamics; lead academic for World Bank WDR 2024 (Middle-Income Trap) and 2026 (AI and development) [Source](https://economics.uchicago.edu/directory/ufuk-akcigit).
- "Attention (And Money) Is All You Need: Why Universities Are Struggling to Keep AI Talent," with Chikis, Dinlersoz & Goldschlag, NBER WP 34964 [Source](http://www.ufukakcigit.com/research-1).
- Co-editor, *The Economics of Creative Destruction* (Harvard UP, 2022) with John Van Reenen.
- **Method:** Macro firm dynamics and creative destruction with large administrative datasets.

### 16. Catherine Tucker — MIT Sloan (Sloan Distinguished Professor of Management) **[F, M]**
Co-director of NBER Economics of Digitization and AI program.
- "Privacy, Algorithms, and AI" in *The Economics of AI: An Agenda* (2019).
- Extensive field experiments on advertising, privacy, and algorithmic fairness (e.g., Lambrecht–Tucker AI gender-targeted ads).
- *Research Handbook on Data, Privacy and Competition Law* (ed., 2025) [Source](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/faculty/directory/catherine-tucker).
- **Method:** Large-scale field experiments and randomized studies in digital platforms.
- **Public engagement:** Testified before US Congress; presents to FTC, FCC, OECD, IMF.

### 17. Tim Bresnahan — Stanford (Emeritus, HAI Affiliate) **[M, F]**
Godfather of the GPT (general-purpose technology) concept (Bresnahan–Trajtenberg 1995).
- "What Innovation Paths for AI to Become a GPT?" *JEMS* (2024) [Source](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jems.12524).
- "Artificial Intelligence Technologies and Aggregate Growth Prospects" (Stanford DEL, 2024).
- **Method:** Industrial organization; historical analogies (computer industry, AI).

### 18. Manuel Trajtenberg — Tel Aviv University; CEPR/NBER **[M, L]**
Co-creator (with Bresnahan) of the GPT concept in economics.
- "AI as the next GPT: A Political-Economy Perspective," NBER WP 24245 (2018) [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w24245).
- Emphasizes policy response: education reform, professionalization of personal-care occupations.

## IV. AI, Labor Markets and Firm Productivity (Empirical Frontier)

### 19. Danielle Li — MIT Sloan (Associate Professor) **[L, F]**
- "Generative AI at Work," with Brynjolfsson & Raymond, *QJE* (2025) — the benchmark field study of GenAI productivity [Source](https://danielle.li/assets/docs/GenerativeAIatWork.pdf).
- Continuing work on AI adoption and diffusion of knowledge through AI systems [Source](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/centers-initiatives/institute-work-and-employment-research/generative-ai-and-worker-productivity).

### 20. Nicholas Bloom — Stanford (William D. Eberle Professor of Economics) **[L, F]**
Measurement-focused macroeconomist.
- "Firm Data on AI," with Yotzov, Barrero, Bunn, Davis et al., NBER WP 34836 (2026) — ~6,000 firms across US, UK, Germany, Australia; 69% of firms use AI but executive usage averages only 1.5 hrs/wk [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34836).
- "Are Ideas Getting Harder to Find?" with C. Jones, Van Reenen & M. Webb, *AER* (2020).
- **Method:** Large firm surveys (Survey of Business Uncertainty) and management practices measurement.

### 21. John Van Reenen — LSE (Ronald Coase Chair) & MIT Digital Fellow **[L, F]**
- Management, innovation and productivity work including "Are Ideas Getting Harder to Find?" (2020, with Bloom, Jones, Webb).
- Co-editor of *The Economics of Creative Destruction* (2022) with Akcigit.
- **Public engagement:** OBE for services to economics; Royal Economic Society Council [Source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Van_Reenen_(economist)).

### 22. David Deming — Harvard Kennedy School → Harvard College Dean (Isabelle & Scott Black Professor of Political Economy) **[E, L, F]**
- "The Rapid Adoption of Generative AI," with Bick & Blandin, NBER WP 32966 (2024) — documents the fastest new-technology diffusion on record [Source](https://www.daviddeming.com/research).
- "The ABCs of Working with AI: Ability, Beliefs, and Calibration," with Caplin et al., *Management Science* (forthcoming).
- "Mind the Gap: AI Adoption in Europe and the US" (2026).
- "Skills and Human Capital in the Labor Market," *Handbook of Labor Economics* (2025).
- **Method:** Applied micro using survey/admin data; now doing work on human leadership skills with AI agents.

### 23. Pamela Mishkin & Tyna Eloundou — OpenAI (formerly/currently) **[L, M]**
With Sam Manning (OpenResearch) and Daniel Rock, authors of the much-cited occupational exposure study.
- "GPTs are GPTs: Labor Market Impact Potential of LLMs," *Science* (June 2024) — ~80% of US workforce has at least 10% of tasks exposed [Source](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adj0998).

### 24. Daniel Rock — Wharton (OID; Assistant Professor) **[L, F, M]**
- "GPTs are GPTs" (with Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin) [Source](https://oid.wharton.upenn.edu/profile/rockdi/).
- "The Productivity J-Curve," with Brynjolfsson & Syverson, *AEJ: Macroeconomics* (2021).
- "AI and the Modern Productivity Paradox" (with Brynjolfsson & Syverson), lead chapter in Agrawal–Gans–Goldfarb NBER volume.
- **Method:** Empirical IO and productivity measurement.

### 25. Shakked Noy & Whitney Zhang — MIT Economics PhD graduates **[L]**
Authors of the iconic ChatGPT RCT.
- "Experimental Evidence on the Productivity Effects of Generative Artificial Intelligence," *Science* (2023) — 444 professionals RCT, 40% time reduction, 18% quality gain, compression of inequality [Source](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adh2586).
- **Method:** Preregistered online RCT with incentivized writing tasks.

### 26. Anders Humlum — University of Chicago Booth (Assistant Professor of Economics) **[L, I]**
Key empirical voice on adoption and real-world impact.
- "The Unequal Adoption of ChatGPT Exacerbates Existing Inequalities Among Workers," with E. Vestergaard, *PNAS* (2025) [Source](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2414972121).
- "Large Language Models, Small Labor Market Effects" (with Vestergaard) — linked Danish register data found small effects on wages/hours [Source](https://www.andershumlum.com/research).
- **Method:** Large-scale survey experiment linked to Danish administrative data.

### 27. Anna Salomons — Tilburg University / Utrecht University School of Economics **[L, I]**
- With Autor, Chin, Seegmiller, "New Frontiers: The Origins and Content of New Work," *QJE* (2024).
- With D. Autor, "Is Automation Labor-Displacing?" *BPEA* (2018).
- 2024 EEA Role Models in Economics Award [Source](https://shapingwork.mit.edu/about-us/anna-salomons/).
- **Method:** Cross-country labor data; task-level analysis.

### 28. Xavier Jaravel — LSE (Professor of Economics) **[L, F, I]**
- "How Different Uses of AI Shape Labor Demand: Evidence from France," with Aghion, Bunel, Mikaelsen, Roulet, Søgaard, *AEA P&P* (2025) [Source](https://eprints.lse.ac.uk/128375/).
- Inflation inequality agenda; Council of Economic Analysis to the French PM; 2019 Philip Leverhulme Prize [Source](https://cep.lse.ac.uk/_new/people/person.asp?id=10583).

### 29. Michael Webb — Stanford (PhD) / DeepMind / UK Government **[L]**
- "The Impact of Artificial Intelligence on the Labor Market" (2019) — patent-text/O*NET exposure method that predicted AI would hit high-skill tasks [Source](https://www.michaelwebb.co/).
- Co-author of "Are Ideas Getting Harder to Find?" (2020).
- **Method:** Novel text-based overlap measures (patents × tasks).

### 30. Ed Felten — Princeton (CITP) with Manav Raj (Wharton) and Robert Seamans (NYU Stern) **[L]**
- "Occupational, Industry, and Geographic Exposure to Artificial Intelligence: A Novel Dataset and Its Potential Uses," *Strategic Management Journal* (2021) [Source](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3822412).
- "How will Language Modelers like ChatGPT Affect Occupations and Industries?" (2023); "Occupational Heterogeneity in Exposure to Generative AI" (2023).
- **Method:** AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE) index (abilities × AI applications).
- **Public engagement:** Felten is a former Deputy US CTO and Federal Trade Commission advisor.

### 31. Carl Benedikt Frey — Oxford (Dieter Schwarz Associate Professor of AI & Work, OII, Mansfield College) **[L, M, I]**
- With M. Osborne, "The Future of Employment" (2013; published 2017) — the single most-cited paper on automation risk [Source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Benedikt_Frey).
- *The Technology Trap* (Princeton UP, 2019); *How Progress Ends* (2025).
- "Generative AI and the Future of Work: A Reappraisal," with Osborne, *Brown Journal of World Affairs* (2024) [Source](https://oms-www.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/academic/2023-FoW-Working-Paper-Generative-AI-and-the-Future-of-Work-A-Reappraisal-combined.pdf).
- **Public engagement:** Advises G20, OECD, EC, UN; wrote for FT, Foreign Affairs, WSJ, NYT.

### 32. Tania Babina — Columbia Business School **[F, L]**
- "Artificial Intelligence, Firm Growth, and Product Innovation," with Fedyk, He & Hodson, *Journal of Financial Economics* (2024) — finds AI adoption drives product innovation and industry concentration, not operational cost cuts [Source](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X).
- "Firm Investments in AI Technologies and Changes in Workforce Composition" (NBER chapter, 2023).
- **Method:** Resume and job-postings data + IV for AI investment.

### 33. Alex Xi He — University of Maryland Smith School **[F, L]**
Same team as Babina; "Artificial Intelligence and Firms' Systematic Risk" (2024), "Firm Investments in AI Technologies and Changes in Workforce Composition" [Source](https://www.alexxihe.com/).

### 34. Iain Cockburn — Boston University Questrom; with Rebecca Henderson (HBS) and Scott Stern (MIT Sloan) **[M, F]**
- "The Impact of Artificial Intelligence on Innovation," NBER WP 24449 (2018) — influential argument that deep learning is a general-purpose method of invention (IMI) [Source](https://www.nber.org/papers/w24449).

### 35. Fabrizio Dell'Acqua & Karim Lakhani — Harvard Business School **[F, L]**
- "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier," with McFowland, Mollick, Kellogg, Lifshitz-Assaf, Candelon et al., *Organization Science* (2026); the BCG field experiment that gave rise to the "centaurs vs. cyborgs" and "jagged frontier" concepts [Source](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/orsc.2025.21838).
- "The Cybernetic Teammate: A Field Experiment on Generative AI and Teamwork" (2025) [Source](https://www.fabriziodellacqua.com/).
- **Method:** Large randomized field experiments inside firms.

### 36. Luis Garicano — LSE School of Public Policy **[L, F]**
- Foundational "Hierarchies and the Organization of Knowledge in Production," *JPE* (2000).
- "Training in the Age of AI: A Theory of Apprenticeship Viability," with L. Rayo (2025–2026).
- "Superabundant AI" chapter for the Agrawal–Korinek–Brynjolfsson volume on Transformative AI.
- **Public engagement:** Former Member of the European Parliament, VP of Renew Europe; blog *Silicon Continent* [Source](https://sites.google.com/site/luisgaricano/ai-research-and-policy).

## V. AI, Education, and Human Capital

### 37. Eric Hanushek — Stanford (Hoover Senior Fellow) **[E]**
Dean of education-economics; 2021 Yidan Prize.
- Framework papers on "Knowledge Capital of Nations" that underpin analyses of how AI tutors might raise human capital [Source](https://www.hoover.org/profiles/eric-hanushek).
- *The Knowledge Capital of Nations: Education and the Economics of Growth* (MIT Press, 2015).
- His production-function framework is now widely used to evaluate AI tutors.

### 38. David Deming (see entry 22) — also central here, especially on skills, college wage premium, and AI tutors **[E]**.

### 39. Raj Chetty — Harvard (William A. Ackman Professor) **[E]**
- Not a primary AI scholar but his course *"Using Big Data to Solve Economic and Social Problems"* (Econ 1152/50) incorporates AI and machine learning; Deming delivered a recent AI lecture in it [Source](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2026/4/14/deming-ai-literacy-ec50/).
- Runs Opportunity Insights, bringing ML methods to mobility research.

### 40. Ethan Mollick — Wharton (Ralph J. Roberts Distinguished Faculty Scholar) **[E, L]**

## How to Read This Map

For a Colombian applied economist interested in **causal inference and the economics of AI**, the most directly relevant nodes are:

- **Causal inference + AI (methods):** Athey, Imbens, Mullainathan, Rambachan.
- **AI and labor markets (field experiments and admin data):** Brynjolfsson–Li–Raymond, Noy–Zhang, Humlum–Vestergaard, Autor, Deming, Bick–Blandin, Aghion–Jaravel–Bunel.
- **Firm-level AI adoption:** Babina, He, McElheran, Bloom, Van Reenen, Brynjolfsson, Acemoglu.
- **Theory / macro / transformative AI:** Acemoglu, Restrepo, Aghion–Jones–Jones, Korinek, Trammell, C. Jones, Garicano.
- **AI and education (with randomized evidence):** Deming, Mullainathan, Mollick, Kestin & Miller, Cowen–Tabarrok, Hanushek's framework.
- **AI, inequality, regulation and the AI industry:** Korinek, Stiglitz, Coyle, Acemoglu–Johnson, Tucker, Crémer, Schnitzer, Farronato.
- **Key hubs to follow:** Stanford Digital Economy Lab (Brynjolfsson), MIT Shaping the Future of Work (Autor, Johnson, Restrepo-affiliated), UVA's EconTAI (Korinek), Rotman's Creative Destruction Lab (Agrawal, Gans, Goldfarb), Oxford Future of Work (Frey), TSE Digital Center (Crémer), LSE POID/CEP (Van Reenen, Jaravel, Garicano), Cambridge Bennett School (Coyle), Becker Friedman Institute at Chicago (Akcigit, Humlum, Mullainathan, Bertrand).

The NBER **Economics of AI conferences** (ongoing since 2017, organized by Agrawal, Gans, Goldfarb, Brynjolfsson, Korinek and Tucker), the **CEPR AI Research Network** (led by Korinek), and the **SF Fed's EmergingTech Economic Research Network (EERN) seminars** are the three most efficient single windows into the research frontier. The 2019 NBER volume *The Economics of Artificial Intelligence: An Agenda* and the 2025 follow-up *The Economics of Transformative AI* (both University of Chicago Press) are the canonical references for entering the field.

*Note on conflicts and caveats:* Estimates of AI's macroeconomic impact vary dramatically across these researchers — Acemoglu's ~0.66% TFP gain over 10 years sits at the conservative end, while Brynjolfsson's "productivity J-curve" perspective, Aghion's electricity-wave analogy (≈0.8–1.3 pp per year), and Korinek–Trammell's transformative-AI scenarios span a much wider range. This divergence reflects genuine uncertainty, not consensus, and any survey of AI economics should explicitly foreground it.
